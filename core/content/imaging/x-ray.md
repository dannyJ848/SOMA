# X-Ray Imaging

## Overview
X-ray imaging (radiography) is the oldest and most widely used medical imaging technique. X-rays pass through the body and create images based on how different tissues absorb radiation. Dense structures like bone absorb more X-rays and appear white, while air-filled structures appear black.

---

## How X-Rays Work

### Level 1: Foundation
X-rays are a type of energy (like light, but invisible) that can pass through your body. A machine sends X-rays through you, and a detector on the other side captures an image.

**Simple analogy**: Think of X-rays like a flashlight shining through your hand - you can see the shadow of your bones because they block more light than your skin.

**Key points**:
- X-rays pass through soft tissues easily
- Bones and dense objects block X-rays
- The image shows shadows of what's inside you
- It only takes seconds to get an X-ray

### Level 2: Core Understanding
X-rays are a form of electromagnetic radiation with high energy that allows them to penetrate body tissues. Different tissues absorb X-rays differently:

**Radiodensity Scale** (white to black):
1. **Metal** - Brightest white (blocks all X-rays)
2. **Bone** - White (high absorption)
3. **Soft tissue/fluid** - Gray (moderate absorption)
4. **Fat** - Darker gray (lower absorption)
5. **Air** - Black (minimal absorption)

**Image Formation**:
- X-ray tube generates radiation
- Beam passes through patient
- Tissues absorb varying amounts
- Remaining rays hit detector/film
- Computer creates image

**Types of X-ray Images**:
- Plain radiographs (single images)
- Fluoroscopy (real-time moving X-rays)
- Contrast studies (with dye to highlight structures)

### Level 3: Clinical Application
**Understanding the Physics**:

**X-ray Production**:
1. Electrons accelerated across high voltage (40-150 kV)
2. Strike tungsten target
3. Produce X-rays (99% heat, 1% X-rays)
4. Filtered and collimated into beam

**Image Quality Factors**:
- **kV (kilovoltage)**: Controls penetration
- **mA (milliamperage)**: Controls quantity of X-rays
- **Time**: Exposure duration
- **Distance**: Affects intensity (inverse square law)

**Patient Factors Affecting Image**:
- Body habitus (size/thickness)
- Position/rotation
- Motion artifact
- Overlying objects (jewelry, clothing)

**Technical Artifacts**:
- Motion blur
- Double exposure
- Grid lines
- Foreign objects
- Underpenetration/overpenetration

### Level 4: Advanced Mechanisms
**Radiation Interactions with Matter**:

1. **Photoelectric Effect**:
   - Photon absorbed completely
   - Produces characteristic "white" appearance
   - Predominates with high atomic number tissues (bone)
   - More common at lower energies

2. **Compton Scattering**:
   - Partial energy transfer
   - Causes image fog/scatter
   - Predominates in soft tissue
   - More common at higher energies

3. **Pair Production** (not relevant at diagnostic energies)

**Beam Characteristics**:
- Polychromatic beam (multiple energies)
- Beam hardening through tissue
- Inverse square law: I = 1/d^2
- Heel effect at anode side

**Digital Radiography**:
- **CR (Computed Radiography)**: Phosphor storage plates
- **DR (Digital Radiography)**: Direct digital detectors
- Advantages: Lower dose, post-processing, PACS storage

### Level 5: Expert Integration
**Advanced Image Optimization**:

**Modulation Transfer Function (MTF)**:
- Measures spatial resolution capability
- Affected by focal spot size, detector resolution
- Higher MTF = better detail resolution

**Detective Quantum Efficiency (DQE)**:
- Efficiency of converting X-ray photons to image
- Higher DQE = lower patient dose possible

**Dose Optimization Strategies**:
1. Automatic Exposure Control (AEC)
2. Collimation to area of interest
3. Appropriate kV/mAs selection
4. Gonadal/thyroid shielding (when appropriate)
5. Grid use for scatter reduction

**Quality Assurance**:
- Daily QA testing
- Phantom imaging
- Reject analysis
- Dose monitoring

## Chest X-Ray (CXR)

### Level 1: Foundation
A chest X-ray is a picture of your heart, lungs, and the bones around them. It's one of the most common medical tests.

**What it can show**:
- Pneumonia (lung infection)
- Heart size
- Broken ribs
- Fluid in or around lungs
- Foreign objects

**What to expect**:
- Stand against a flat surface
- Take a deep breath and hold it
- Stay very still for a few seconds
- Usually two pictures are taken (front and side)

**Normal appearance**:
- Lungs should look black (filled with air)
- Heart is in the left-center
- Ribs and spine appear white
- Diaphragm separates chest from abdomen

### Level 2: Core Understanding
**Standard Views**:

1. **PA (Posteroanterior)**:
   - X-rays enter back, exit front
   - Standard view, patient facing detector
   - Heart size most accurate

2. **Lateral**:
   - Left side against detector
   - Shows structures behind heart
   - Helps localize abnormalities

3. **AP (Anteroposterior)**:
   - Portable/bedside X-rays
   - Heart appears magnified
   - Lower quality than PA

**Systematic Review - "ABCDEFGHI"**:
- **A**irway: Trachea, bronchi
- **B**ones: Ribs, spine, clavicles
- **C**ardiac: Heart size and shape
- **D**iaphragm: Position, costophrenic angles
- **E**ffusion/edges: Pleural fluid, lung margins
- **F**ields: Lung tissue
- **G**astric bubble: Below left diaphragm
- **H**ila: Pulmonary vessels
- **I**nstruments: Lines, tubes, pacemakers

**Normal Measurements**:
- Cardiothoracic ratio: <50% on PA film
- Trachea: Midline or slightly right
- Diaphragm: Right higher than left (liver)
- Costophrenic angles: Sharp

### Level 3: Clinical Application
**Common Abnormal Findings**:

**Lung Opacities**:
| Pattern | Appearance | Common Causes |
|---------|------------|---------------|
| Consolidation | Dense white, air bronchograms | Pneumonia, hemorrhage |
| Ground glass | Hazy, can see vessels through | Viral infection, edema |
| Nodule | Round, <3cm | Cancer, granuloma |
| Mass | Round, >3cm | Cancer, abscess |
| Interstitial | Lines, reticular | Fibrosis, CHF |

**Silhouette Sign**:
- Loss of normal border indicates adjacent pathology
- Right heart border: Right middle lobe
- Left heart border: Lingula
- Diaphragm: Lower lobes

**Air Bronchograms**:
- Dark branching airways visible within white lung
- Indicates alveolar (not bronchial) pathology
- Classic for pneumonia

**Pleural Abnormalities**:
- Effusion: Blunting of costophrenic angle
- Pneumothorax: Absent lung markings, visible pleural line
- Tension pneumothorax: Mediastinal shift away

**Cardiac Abnormalities**:
- Cardiomegaly: CTR >50%
- Left atrial enlargement: Double density, elevated bronchus
- Pulmonary edema: Bat wing opacity, Kerley B lines

### Level 4: Advanced Mechanisms
**Radiographic Anatomy Deep Dive**:

**Mediastinal Compartments** (lateral view):
1. **Anterior**: Thymus, ascending aorta, lymph nodes
2. **Middle**: Heart, trachea, main bronchi
3. **Posterior**: Descending aorta, esophagus, spine

**Hilar Anatomy**:
- Left hilum: 0.5-1.5 cm higher than right
- Main pulmonary arteries
- Main bronchi
- Lymph nodes (not normally visible)

**Fissures**:
- Major (oblique) fissures: Separate lower lobes
- Minor (horizontal) fissure: Right side only, separates RML from RUL
- Accessory fissures: Anatomic variants

**Critical Lines and Tubes**:
| Device | Ideal Position |
|--------|----------------|
| ETT | 3-5 cm above carina |
| Central line | SVC, above right atrium |
| NG tube | Below diaphragm |
| Chest tube | Apex for PTX, base for effusion |
| PICC | SVC or brachiocephalic vein |
| Swan-Ganz | Main PA, <2 cm past hilum |

### Level 5: Expert Integration
**Pattern Recognition Mastery**:

**Classic Radiographic Signs**:
- **Hampton's hump**: Wedge-shaped opacity (PE with infarction)
- **Westermark sign**: Focal oligemia (PE)
- **Golden S sign**: Central mass with collapse (bronchogenic Ca)
- **Sail sign**: Thymic shadow (normal in children)
- **Deep sulcus sign**: Supine pneumothorax
- **Continuous diaphragm sign**: Pneumomediastinum

**Differential Diagnosis by Location**:

**Upper Zone Diseases**:
- TB (reactivation)
- Sarcoidosis
- Silicosis
- Ankylosing spondylitis
- COPF (Coal workers)

**Lower Zone Diseases**:
- Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis
- Asbestosis
- Rheumatoid lung
- Aspiration

**Integration with Clinical Findings**:
- Correlate with symptoms, labs, vitals
- Comparison with prior imaging essential
- Know limitations (up to 200mL effusion not visible)
- Consider clinical pretest probability

## Bone X-Rays

### Level 1: Foundation
Bone X-rays help doctors see if bones are broken, infected, or have other problems. Bones show up white on X-rays because they're dense.

**Common uses**:
- Check for fractures (broken bones)
- Look for arthritis
- Find infections
- Monitor healing
- Evaluate alignment

**What to expect**:
- Remove jewelry and clothing over the area
- Position the body part on the detector
- Stay very still
- May need several views

### Level 2: Core Understanding
**Standard Views - "Rule of Twos"**:
- **Two views**: At least 90 degrees apart (AP and lateral)
- **Two joints**: Include joints above and below injury
- **Two times**: Compare with prior images if available
- **Two sides**: Compare with opposite limb if needed

**Basic Fracture Description**:
1. **Location**: Which bone, what part (proximal, mid, distal)
2. **Type**: Complete, incomplete, comminuted
3. **Displacement**: Shifted, angulated, rotated
4. **Alignment**: Relationship of fragments
5. **Associated findings**: Soft tissue swelling, joint effusion

**Pediatric Considerations**:
- Growth plates (physis) visible as dark lines
- Bones are more porous, bend more easily
- Salter-Harris classification for growth plate injuries
- Compare with opposite side for normal variants

### Level 3: Clinical Application
**Fracture Patterns and Mechanisms**:

| Fracture Type | Description | Common Cause |
|---------------|-------------|--------------|
| Transverse | Perpendicular to bone | Direct blow |
| Oblique | Diagonal across bone | Rotational force |
| Spiral | Twisting pattern | Rotational force |
| Comminuted | Multiple fragments | High-energy trauma |
| Avulsion | Fragment pulled off | Tendon/ligament tension |
| Compression | Bone crushed | Axial loading |
| Pathologic | Through abnormal bone | Tumor, osteoporosis |
| Stress | Overuse injury | Repetitive loading |

**Common Missed Fractures**:
- Scaphoid (wrist)
- Radial head (elbow)
- Lisfranc (foot)
- Cervical spine
- Posterior malleolus (ankle)

**Arthritis Patterns**:

**Osteoarthritis (OA)**:
- Joint space narrowing
- Subchondral sclerosis
- Osteophytes (bone spurs)
- Subchondral cysts
- DIP/PIP involvement, spares MCP

**Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)**:
- Periarticular erosions
- Joint space narrowing
- Periarticular osteopenia
- MCP/PIP involvement, spares DIP
- Symmetric distribution

### Level 4: Advanced Mechanisms
**Salter-Harris Classification** (Pediatric Growth Plate Injuries):

| Type | Description | Prognosis |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| I | Through physis only | Excellent |
| II | Through physis + metaphysis | Good |
| III | Through physis + epiphysis | Fair |
| IV | Through metaphysis + physis + epiphysis | Guarded |
| V | Crush injury to physis | Poor |

**Mnemonic**: SALTR (Same, Above, Lower, Through, Ruined)

**Bone Tumors - Radiographic Features**:

**Benign Characteristics**:
- Well-defined margins
- Geographic pattern
- Narrow zone of transition
- Sclerotic rim
- No periosteal reaction

**Malignant Characteristics**:
- Ill-defined margins
- Permeative/moth-eaten pattern
- Wide zone of transition
- Cortical destruction
- Aggressive periosteal reaction (sunburst, onion skin)

**Common Bone Lesions by Age**:
- Children: Ewing sarcoma, osteosarcoma
- 20-40: Giant cell tumor, chondroblastoma
- >40: Metastases, myeloma, chondrosarcoma

### Level 5: Expert Integration
**Complex Fracture Management**:

**Stability Assessment**:
Spine fractures - "Three Column Model":
- Anterior: Anterior 2/3 of vertebral body
- Middle: Posterior 1/3 body + posterior longitudinal ligament
- Posterior: Neural arch + ligaments
- Unstable if 2+ columns disrupted

**Healing Assessment**:
| Stage | Timeframe | X-ray Findings |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Inflammatory | 0-2 weeks | Soft tissue swelling |
| Soft callus | 2-6 weeks | Periosteal reaction |
| Hard callus | 6-12 weeks | Calcifying callus |
| Remodeling | Months-years | Normal contour restoration |

**Complications**:
- Delayed union: Healing slower than expected
- Nonunion: Failure to heal (>6-9 months)
- Malunion: Healed in abnormal position
- Avascular necrosis: Loss of blood supply
- Post-traumatic arthritis

## Abdominal X-Ray (AXR)

### Level 1: Foundation
An abdominal X-ray shows the stomach, intestines, and solid organs in your belly. It's often used when you have belly pain.

**What it can show**:
- Bowel obstruction (blockage)
- Swallowed objects
- Kidney stones (some types)
- Free air (perforated bowel)
- Constipation

**What to expect**:
- Lie flat on your back
- May need standing or side-lying views
- Quick, painless test
- Remove belts and metal objects

### Level 2: Core Understanding
**Standard Views**:

1. **Supine (KUB)**: Kidneys, Ureters, Bladder
   - Routine view
   - Good for bowel gas pattern, calcifications

2. **Upright/Erect**:
   - Shows air-fluid levels
   - Free air under diaphragm
   - Better for obstruction

3. **Left Lateral Decubitus**:
   - Patient lies on left side
   - Alternative when can't stand
   - Detects small amounts of free air

**Normal Findings**:
- Small amount of bowel gas
- No dilated loops
- Solid organ shadows (liver, spleen, kidneys)
- Psoas muscle margins visible
- No free air

**Gas Patterns**:
- Normal: Small amounts scattered
- Small bowel: Central, valvulae conniventes (cross entire lumen)
- Large bowel: Peripheral, haustrae (don't cross entire lumen)
- Stomach: Left upper quadrant

### Level 3: Clinical Application
**Bowel Obstruction**:

**Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)**:
- Dilated small bowel (>3 cm)
- Multiple air-fluid levels
- Collapsed distal bowel
- Central location
- Valvulae conniventes visible
- Causes: Adhesions, hernias, tumors

**Large Bowel Obstruction (LBO)**:
- Dilated colon (>6 cm, cecum >9 cm)
- Peripheral location
- Haustral markings
- Competent ileocecal valve: No small bowel dilation
- Causes: Cancer, volvulus, diverticulitis

**Sigmoid Volvulus**:
- "Coffee bean" or "bent inner tube" sign
- Large loop pointing toward right upper quadrant
- No haustral markings

**Cecal Volvulus**:
- Dilated cecum in abnormal location
- Often left upper quadrant

**Free Air (Pneumoperitoneum)**:
- Upright: Under diaphragm
- Supine: Football sign, Rigler's sign (double wall)
- Indicates perforation - surgical emergency

### Level 4: Advanced Mechanisms
**Systematic Review - "GO GAS"**:
- **G**as pattern: Bowel dilation, location
- **O**utline: Organs, psoas, bladder
- **G**ranu: Calcifications, stones
- **A**rtifacts: Lines, tubes, foreign bodies
- **S**keletal: Spine, pelvis, ribs

**Calcifications by Location**:

| Location | Structure | Significance |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| RUQ | Gallstones | Only 10-15% visible |
| Flank | Kidney/ureteral stones | 90% visible |
| Pelvis | Bladder stones, phleboliths | Phleboliths: round, lucent center |
| Vascular | Aortic calcification | Atherosclerosis |
| Pancreatic | Chronic pancreatitis | Midline, upper abdomen |

**Ileus vs. Obstruction**:

| Feature | Ileus | Mechanical Obstruction |
|---------|-------|----------------------|
| Gas distribution | Throughout GI tract | Proximal to point of obstruction |
| Transition point | No | Yes |
| Air-fluid levels | Few, same level | Multiple, different levels |
| Bowel sounds | Decreased | High-pitched initially |

### Level 5: Expert Integration
**Critical Findings - "Spot the Emergency"**:

1. **Toxic Megacolon**:
   - Transverse colon >6 cm
   - Loss of haustral markings
   - Thumbprinting (mucosal edema)
   - Clinical: Fever, tachycardia, IBD history

2. **Pneumatosis Intestinalis**:
   - Air within bowel wall
   - Linear lucencies along bowel
   - May indicate ischemia

3. **Portal Venous Gas**:
   - Branching linear lucencies over liver
   - Peripheral distribution
   - Ominous sign of bowel necrosis

4. **Massive Gastric Dilation**:
   - Risk of aspiration
   - May require NG decompression

**Appendicitis on Plain Film**:
- Fecalith in RLQ (seen in ~10%)
- Sentinel loop (localized ileus)
- Loss of psoas margin
- Scoliosis (pain reflex)
- Usually requires CT for diagnosis

## Radiation Safety

### Level 1: Foundation
X-rays use a small amount of radiation to create pictures. The amount is very small and generally safe, but doctors only order X-rays when the benefits outweigh any tiny risk.

**Key points**:
- Radiation dose is measured carefully
- Modern machines use the lowest possible dose
- Lead shields protect sensitive areas when possible
- The risk from one X-ray is extremely small
- Always tell staff if you might be pregnant

**Comparison**:
- Chest X-ray: About 3 days of natural background radiation
- Flying coast-to-coast: Similar to a chest X-ray

### Level 2: Core Understanding
**Radiation Units**:

| Unit | Measures | Old Unit |
|------|----------|----------|
| Gray (Gy) | Absorbed dose | rad |
| Sievert (Sv) | Effective dose (biological effect) | rem |
| Millisievert (mSv) | 1/1000 of Sievert | mrem |

**Common Radiation Doses**:

| Source | Dose (mSv) | Equivalent Background |
|--------|------------|----------------------|
| Chest X-ray | 0.1 | 10 days |
| Extremity X-ray | 0.001-0.01 | Hours to 1 day |
| Lumbar spine | 1.5 | 6 months |
| Abdominal X-ray | 0.7 | 3 months |
| Background radiation/year | 3.0 | - |

**ALARA Principle**: As Low As Reasonably Achievable
- Minimize time
- Maximize distance
- Use appropriate shielding

### Level 3: Clinical Application
**Radiation Risk Perspective**:

**Stochastic Effects** (chance-based, no threshold):
- Cancer risk
- Genetic effects
- Risk increases with dose
- No "safe" threshold theoretically

**Deterministic Effects** (dose-dependent threshold):
- Skin reddening (>2 Gy)
- Hair loss (>3 Gy)
- Cataracts (>0.5 Gy)
- Not seen at diagnostic doses

**Pregnancy Considerations**:
- Fetal dose <50 mSv: No measurable risk increase
- Most diagnostic exams: Far below this threshold
- Shield pelvis when possible
- If exam essential, benefits usually outweigh risks
- Always inform staff of possible pregnancy

**Pediatric Considerations**:
- Children more radiosensitive
- Longer lifetime for effects to manifest
- "Image Gently" campaign
- Use pediatric protocols

### Level 4: Advanced Mechanisms
**Linear No-Threshold (LNT) Model**:
- Assumes any radiation has some risk
- Risk proportional to dose
- Conservative model for radiation protection
- Basis for regulatory limits

**Cancer Risk Estimates**:
- 5% per Sievert (whole body exposure)
- One chest X-ray: ~1 in 1,000,000 additional cancer risk
- CT abdomen: ~1 in 2,000 additional cancer risk

**Occupational Limits**:
- Annual limit: 50 mSv
- 5-year average: 20 mSv/year
- Lens of eye: 20 mSv/year
- Pregnant worker (fetus): 1 mSv during pregnancy

**Dose Reduction Strategies**:
1. Clinical justification (order only when needed)
2. Optimization (lowest dose for diagnostic quality)
3. Collimation (smaller field = less dose)
4. Automatic exposure control
5. High-speed image receptors
6. Appropriate technical factors

### Level 5: Expert Integration
**Benefit-Risk Analysis**:

**When to Image**:
- Clear clinical indication
- Will change management
- Benefits outweigh risks
- No better alternative

**Radiation Dose in Perspective**:

| Event | Risk of Death |
|-------|---------------|
| Smoking 10 cigarettes | 1 in 200 |
| Driving 60 miles | 1 in 1,000,000 |
| Chest X-ray (cancer) | 1 in 1,000,000 |
| Flying 2500 miles | 1 in 2,000,000 |

**Cumulative Dose Tracking**:
- Important for repeat imaging patients
- Fluoroscopy procedures can add up
- CT is main contributor to medical radiation
- Consider alternative modalities (US, MRI)

## Preparing for Your X-Ray

### What to Wear
- Loose, comfortable clothing without metal
- You may be given a gown
- Remove jewelry, glasses, hearing aids if in imaging area

### What to Tell the Technologist
- If you might be pregnant
- Prior surgeries or implants
- Difficulty lying flat or standing
- Allergies (for contrast studies)

### During the Exam
- Follow positioning instructions
- Take a deep breath and hold when asked
- Stay completely still
- The exam takes only minutes

### After the Exam
- No restrictions for plain X-rays
- Results typically available within hours to a day
- Your doctor will explain the findings

## Understanding Your X-Ray Report

### Common Terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Opacity | White area (can be normal or abnormal) |
| Lucency | Dark area |
| Consolidation | Lung filled with fluid/pus instead of air |
| Effusion | Fluid collection |
| Fracture | Break in bone |
| Dislocation | Joint out of place |
| Degenerative changes | Wear-and-tear changes |
| No acute findings | Nothing immediately dangerous |

### What Reports Include
1. Type of exam and indication
2. Comparison with prior studies
3. Technique/views obtained
4. Findings (systematic description)
5. Impression (summary and conclusions)

### Questions to Ask Your Doctor
- What did the X-ray show?
- Do I need additional imaging?
- What's the next step?
- When should I follow up?

---

## Key Clinical Pearls

1. **Always get two views**: Pathology can be hidden on a single view
2. **Compare to prior images**: Many findings are chronic and unchanged
3. **Clinical correlation essential**: X-ray is one piece of the puzzle
4. **Know the limitations**: Normal X-ray doesn't rule out all pathology
5. **ALARA**: Only order when results will change management
6. **Check the soft tissues**: Don't focus only on bones
7. **Pregnancy**: Always ask, always shield when appropriate
8. **Pediatric patients**: Use lowest possible dose, age-appropriate protocols

---

## Summary

X-ray imaging remains the cornerstone of diagnostic radiology due to its speed, availability, low cost, and relatively low radiation dose. Understanding the physics, systematic interpretation, and clinical applications enables appropriate utilization and accurate diagnosis. While newer modalities offer advanced capabilities, plain radiography continues to be the first-line imaging for many clinical scenarios.
