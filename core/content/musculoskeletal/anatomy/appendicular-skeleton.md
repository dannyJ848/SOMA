# Appendicular Skeleton

## Overview
The appendicular skeleton consists of 126 bones that form the limbs and their attachments to the axial skeleton. It enables locomotion and manipulation of objects. The upper limb (64 bones) attaches via the pectoral girdle, while the lower limb (62 bones) attaches via the pelvic girdle.

---

## Pectoral (Shoulder) Girdle

### Level 1: Foundation
The pectoral girdle connects the upper limb to the axial skeleton. It consists of:
- **Clavicle**: Collar bone, connects sternum to scapula
- **Scapula**: Shoulder blade, provides muscle attachments

This girdle allows great mobility of the upper limb.

### Level 2: Core Understanding
**Clavicle:**
- S-shaped bone
- Only bony attachment of upper limb to axial skeleton
- Medial 2/3: Convex anteriorly
- Lateral 1/3: Concave anteriorly
- Most commonly fractured bone
- Junction of medial and lateral thirds is weakest point

**Scapula:**
- Triangular flat bone
- Key features:
  - Spine: Horizontal ridge on posterior surface
  - Acromion: Lateral end of spine
  - Coracoid process: Anterior projection (muscle attachments)
  - Glenoid cavity: Articulation with humeral head
  - Supraspinous fossa: Above spine (supraspinatus)
  - Infraspinous fossa: Below spine (infraspinatus)
  - Subscapular fossa: Anterior surface (subscapularis)

### Level 3: Clinical Application
**Clavicle Fractures:**
- Most common: Middle third (80%)
- Mechanism: Fall on outstretched hand or direct trauma
- Medial fragment elevated by sternocleidomastoid
- Lateral fragment depressed by arm weight

**Acromioclavicular (AC) Joint:**
- Synovial joint between acromion and clavicle
- Stabilized by AC and coracoclavicular ligaments
- AC separation grades I-VI based on ligament injury

**Scapular Winging:**
- Long thoracic nerve injury → Serratus anterior weakness
- Medial border protrudes when pushing against wall
- May occur with axillary node dissection

### Level 4: Advanced Mechanisms
**Sternoclavicular Joint:**
- Only true joint between upper limb and axial skeleton
- Saddle-type synovial joint
- Articular disc absorbs forces
- Strong capsular and sternoclavicular ligaments
- Posterior dislocation can compress great vessels

**Scapulothoracic Articulation:**
- Not a true joint (no synovial cavity)
- Scapula glides on serratus anterior and subscapularis
- Allows protraction, retraction, elevation, depression, rotation

**Muscle Attachments:**

| Muscle | Origin | Insertion |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| Trapezius | Skull, spinous processes | Clavicle, acromion, scapular spine |
| Levator scapulae | C1-C4 transverse processes | Superior angle of scapula |
| Rhomboid minor | C7-T1 spinous processes | Medial scapular border |
| Rhomboid major | T2-T5 spinous processes | Medial scapular border |
| Serratus anterior | Ribs 1-8 | Medial scapular border (anterior) |
| Pectoralis minor | Ribs 3-5 | Coracoid process |

### Level 5: Expert Integration
**Scapular Kinematics:**
- 2:1 ratio of glenohumeral to scapulothoracic motion
- Full arm abduction: 120° GH + 60° scapular rotation
- Scapular dyskinesia: Altered motion patterns, shoulder pathology

**Brachial Plexus Relations:**
- Clavicle overlies brachial plexus
- Clavicle fracture can injure plexus (rare)
- Cords form around axillary artery

## Upper Limb Bones

### Level 1: Foundation
The upper limb contains 30 bones per side:
- **Humerus**: Arm bone
- **Radius and Ulna**: Forearm bones
- **Carpals (8)**: Wrist bones
- **Metacarpals (5)**: Palm bones
- **Phalanges (14)**: Finger bones

### Level 2: Core Understanding
**Humerus:**
- Head: Articulates with glenoid cavity
- Greater tubercle: Lateral, supraspinatus/infraspinatus/teres minor insert
- Lesser tubercle: Anterior, subscapularis inserts
- Intertubercular (bicipital) groove: Long head of biceps
- Surgical neck: Common fracture site
- Deltoid tuberosity: Lateral shaft, deltoid insertion
- Radial (spiral) groove: Posterior shaft, radial nerve
- Medial and lateral epicondyles: Muscle origins
- Capitulum: Articulates with radius
- Trochlea: Articulates with ulna
- Olecranon fossa: Receives olecranon in extension

**Radius:**
- Head: Articulates with capitulum
- Radial tuberosity: Biceps insertion
- Styloid process: Distal lateral projection
- Ulnar notch: Articulates with ulna distally

**Ulna:**
- Olecranon: Proximal posterior projection
- Coronoid process: Anterior projection
- Trochlear notch: Articulates with trochlea
- Radial notch: Articulates with radial head
- Styloid process: Distal medial projection

### Level 3: Clinical Application
**Humeral Fractures:**
- Surgical neck: Axillary nerve at risk
- Shaft (spiral groove): Radial nerve injury
- Supracondylar: Brachial artery, median nerve at risk

**Forearm Fractures:**
- Colles fracture: Distal radius, dorsal displacement ("dinner fork")
- Smith fracture: Distal radius, volar displacement
- Monteggia: Proximal ulna fracture + radial head dislocation
- Galeazzi: Distal radius fracture + distal radioulnar dislocation

**Nerve-Bone Relationships:**

| Nerve | Bone/Structure | Injury Consequence |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Axillary | Surgical neck humerus | Deltoid weakness, sensory loss |
| Radial | Spiral groove | Wrist drop |
| Median | Supracondylar humerus | Hand weakness, sensory loss |
| Ulnar | Medial epicondyle | Claw hand, sensory loss |

### Level 4: Advanced Mechanisms
**Carpal Bones:**
Proximal row (lateral to medial): **S**ome **L**overs **T**ry **P**ositions
- Scaphoid, Lunate, Triquetrum, Pisiform

Distal row: **T**hat **T**hey **C**an't **H**andle
- Trapezium, Trapezoid, Capitate, Hamate

**Scaphoid:**
- Most commonly fractured carpal
- Blood supply enters distally (retrograde)
- Avascular necrosis risk with proximal fractures
- Snuffbox tenderness: Scaphoid fracture until proven otherwise

**Carpal Tunnel Boundaries:**
- Floor: Carpal bones
- Roof: Flexor retinaculum
- Contents: Median nerve, 9 flexor tendons

**Elbow Joint Complex:**
1. Humeroulnar: Hinge (flexion/extension)
2. Humeroradial: Hinge (flexion/extension)
3. Proximal radioulnar: Pivot (pronation/supination)

### Level 5: Expert Integration
**Carrying Angle:**
- Valgus angle at elbow (5-15°)
- Greater in females
- Allows arms to clear hips during walking
- Increased in cubitus valgus (ulnar nerve stretch)
- Decreased in cubitus varus (gunstock deformity)

**Interosseous Membrane:**
- Connects radius and ulna
- Fibers run inferiomedially from radius to ulna
- Transmits forces from hand to ulna and humerus
- Essex-Lopresti injury: Radial head fracture + IOM rupture + DRUJ instability

**Hand Arches:**
- Longitudinal arch: Metacarpals and phalanges
- Transverse arch (proximal): Carpals
- Transverse arch (distal): Metacarpal heads
- Critical for grip function

## Pelvic Girdle

### Level 1: Foundation
The pelvic girdle connects the lower limb to the axial skeleton. It consists of:
- Two hip bones (os coxae)
- Sacrum and coccyx (axial skeleton)

The pelvis supports body weight and protects pelvic organs.

### Level 2: Core Understanding
**Hip Bone (Os Coxae):**
Three bones fused at acetabulum:
- **Ilium**: Superior, forms iliac crest
- **Ischium**: Posteroinferior, forms ischial tuberosity
- **Pubis**: Anteromedial, forms pubic symphysis

**Key Landmarks:**
- Iliac crest: Superior border
- ASIS/AIIS: Anterior superior/inferior iliac spines
- PSIS/PIIS: Posterior superior/inferior iliac spines
- Acetabulum: Socket for femoral head
- Obturator foramen: Large opening between pubis and ischium
- Ischial tuberosity: "Sit bones"
- Pubic symphysis: Fibrocartilaginous joint anteriorly

### Level 3: Clinical Application
**Pelvic Types (Obstetric):**
- **Gynecoid**: Round inlet, most common in females, favorable
- **Android**: Heart-shaped inlet, more common in males
- **Anthropoid**: Oval (AP elongated)
- **Platypelloid**: Flat (transversely wide)

**Pelvic Measurements:**
- True conjugate: 10 cm (sacral promontory to pubis)
- Diagonal conjugate: 12.5 cm (measurable clinically)
- Interspinous diameter: 10 cm (narrowest)

**Pelvic Fractures:**
- High-energy trauma
- Associated with hemorrhage, urologic injury
- Open book: Anterior compression
- Lateral compression: Most common
- Vertical shear: Highest mortality

**Greater Sciatic Foramen Contents:**
- Piriformis muscle
- Superior gluteal vessels and nerve (above piriformis)
- Inferior gluteal vessels and nerve
- Sciatic nerve
- Pudendal nerve
- Posterior femoral cutaneous nerve

### Level 4: Advanced Mechanisms
**Sacroiliac Joint:**
- Synovial anteriorly, syndesmosis posteriorly
- Strong ligaments: Anterior/posterior SI, iliolumbar, sacrospinous, sacrotuberous
- Slight movement allows force transmission
- SI joint dysfunction: Common cause of low back pain

**Pubic Symphysis:**
- Secondary cartilaginous joint (fibrocartilage)
- Slight movement, increases in pregnancy
- Symphysis pubis dysfunction: Pregnancy-related pain

**Ligaments Creating Foramina:**
- Sacrospinous ligament: Converts greater sciatic notch to foramen
- Sacrotuberous ligament: Creates lesser sciatic foramen

### Level 5: Expert Integration
**Pelvic Ring Stability:**
- Posterior ligaments most important for stability
- Intact posterior ring = stable pelvis
- Disrupted posterior ring = unstable, high mortality

**Pelvic Tilt:**
- Anterior tilt: Increases lumbar lordosis
- Posterior tilt: Flattens lumbar spine
- Affects standing posture and biomechanics

**Avulsion Fractures in Athletes:**
- ASIS: Sartorius (sprinting)
- AIIS: Rectus femoris (kicking)
- Ischial tuberosity: Hamstrings (hurdling)

## Lower Limb Bones

### Level 1: Foundation
The lower limb contains 30 bones per side:
- **Femur**: Thigh bone (longest bone)
- **Patella**: Kneecap
- **Tibia and Fibula**: Leg bones
- **Tarsals (7)**: Ankle bones
- **Metatarsals (5)**: Foot bones
- **Phalanges (14)**: Toe bones

### Level 2: Core Understanding
**Femur:**
- Head: Articulates with acetabulum
- Fovea capitis: Ligament of head attachment
- Neck: Common fracture site
- Greater trochanter: Lateral, gluteal muscle attachment
- Lesser trochanter: Medial, iliopsoas insertion
- Intertrochanteric line: Anterior
- Intertrochanteric crest: Posterior
- Linea aspera: Posterior shaft, muscle attachments
- Medial and lateral condyles: Articulate with tibia
- Patellar surface: Anterior, patella glides here

**Patella:**
- Largest sesamoid bone
- Within quadriceps tendon
- Protects knee, increases quadriceps lever arm

**Tibia:**
- Medial and lateral condyles: Articulate with femur
- Intercondylar eminence: Between condyles
- Tibial tuberosity: Patellar tendon insertion
- Medial malleolus: Distal medial projection
- Subcutaneous (vulnerable to open fractures)

**Fibula:**
- Head: Articulates with tibia
- Common peroneal nerve wraps around neck
- Lateral malleolus: Distal lateral projection

### Level 3: Clinical Application
**Hip Fractures:**
- Femoral neck fractures: Disrupt blood supply, AVN risk
- Intertrochanteric: Extracapsular, better blood supply
- Subtrochanteric: High stress area, difficult healing

**Blood Supply to Femoral Head:**
- Medial femoral circumflex artery (main supply)
- Lateral femoral circumflex artery
- Artery of ligamentum teres (minor in adults)
- Intracapsular fractures disrupt blood supply

**Angle of Inclination:**
- Normal: 125-135°
- Coxa vara: <125° (decreased)
- Coxa valga: >135° (increased)

**Angle of Anteversion:**
- Normal: 10-15°
- Femoral head points anteriorly relative to condyles

**Knee Injuries:**
- Tibial plateau fractures: High-energy trauma
- Patellar fractures: Direct trauma, loss of extension
- Osgood-Schlatter: Tibial tuberosity apophysitis

### Level 4: Advanced Mechanisms
**Tarsal Bones:**
- **Talus**: Articulates with tibia/fibula (ankle mortise)
- **Calcaneus**: Heel bone, largest tarsal
- **Navicular**: Articulates with talus and cuneiforms
- **Cuboid**: Lateral, articulates with metatarsals 4-5
- **Cuneiforms** (3): Medial, intermediate, lateral

**Foot Arches:**
- Medial longitudinal: Highest, most important
- Lateral longitudinal: Lower, more stable
- Transverse: Across cuneiforms and cuboid

**Arch Support:**
- Bones: Keystone shape
- Ligaments: Plantar calcaneonavicular (spring), plantar aponeurosis
- Muscles: Tibialis posterior, peroneus longus

**Ankle Joint:**
- Mortise formed by tibia and fibula
- Talus articulates in mortise
- Talar dome wider anteriorly
- Dorsiflexion: More stable (wider talus engaged)
- Plantarflexion: Less stable (inversion sprains common)

### Level 5: Expert Integration
**Lower Limb Alignment:**
- Mechanical axis: Center of hip → center of knee → center of ankle
- Anatomical axis: Femoral and tibial shaft axes
- Normal valgus alignment at knee: 5-7°

**Malalignment:**
- Genu valgum (knock-knee): Lateral knee loading
- Genu varum (bow-leg): Medial knee loading
- Associated with OA progression

**Fracture Patterns:**
- Maisonneuve: Proximal fibula fracture + syndesmosis injury
- Pilon: Distal tibia articular fracture
- Lisfranc: Tarsometatarsal dislocation
- Jones: 5th metatarsal base fracture

**Gait Cycle Considerations:**
- Heel strike to toe-off: Stance phase (60%)
- Toe-off to heel strike: Swing phase (40%)
- Double support: Both feet on ground
- Lower limb bones aligned for weight transmission

---

## Key Clinical Pearls

1. **Clavicle fractures**: Most common fracture; middle third most common
2. **Scaphoid fractures**: Snuffbox tenderness; AVN risk with proximal fractures
3. **Hip fractures**: Intracapsular disrupts blood supply; AVN risk
4. **Femoral neck angle**: Coxa vara/valga affects biomechanics
5. **Ankle stability**: Greater in dorsiflexion; inversion sprains in plantarflexion

---

## Summary

The appendicular skeleton provides the framework for movement and manipulation. The upper limb, attached loosely via the pectoral girdle, prioritizes mobility for fine motor tasks. The lower limb, attached firmly via the pelvic girdle, prioritizes stability for weight-bearing and locomotion. Understanding the detailed anatomy of these bones and their relationships to neurovascular structures is essential for managing traumatic injuries and understanding biomechanical principles.
