# During the Visit

*Making the most of your time with your healthcare provider.*

---

## Describing Symptoms Effectively

### Complexity Level 1 (Ages 5-8)
When the doctor asks how you feel, you can use special words to help explain:

**Where Does It Hurt?**
- Point to the spot!
- Is it in one place or does it move around?
- Touch your tummy, head, or wherever it feels bad

**How Much Does It Hurt?**
- Use the faces chart: smiley face means no pain, crying face means lots of pain
- Show with your fingers: a little space means a little pain, big space means big pain
- Use words like "a little," "medium," or "a lot"

**What Does It Feel Like?**
- "It feels like something is poking me"
- "It feels hot" or "It feels cold"
- "It feels like butterflies in my tummy"
- "It's an ouchie" or "It's a boo-boo"

**When Does It Hurt?**
- "It hurts all the time"
- "It hurts when I run"
- "It hurts after I eat"
- "It hurts at nighttime"

**Helpful Hint**: It's okay if you don't know the right words. Just describe it the best you can, and the doctor will ask more questions to understand.

---

### Complexity Level 2 (Ages 9-12)
Doctors need specific information to figure out what's wrong. The better you describe your symptoms, the better they can help you.

**The Five W's of Symptoms**:

| Question | What to Tell the Doctor |
|----------|------------------------|
| **What?** | What exactly hurts or feels wrong |
| **Where?** | The exact spot (or spots) on your body |
| **When?** | When it started and when it happens |
| **How bad?** | Rate it 1-10 (10 is the worst) |
| **What else?** | Other things you notice at the same time |

**Describing Pain Types**:
- **Sharp**: Like being poked with something pointy
- **Dull**: Like a heavy, achy feeling
- **Burning**: Like you touched something hot
- **Throbbing**: Like a heartbeat in that spot
- **Cramping**: Like muscles squeezing tight
- **Stabbing**: Like sudden pokes that come and go

**Be Specific About Timing**:
- "It started last Tuesday"
- "It happens every morning when I wake up"
- "It lasts about 30 minutes"
- "It comes and goes throughout the day"
- "It's worse after soccer practice"

**What Makes It Change?**
- What makes it worse? (moving, eating, standing, stress)
- What makes it better? (rest, ice, medicine, lying down)

**Example**: Instead of saying "My stomach hurts," try: "I have a crampy pain in my lower belly that started three days ago. It happens mostly after I eat and feels like a 5 out of 10. Lying down helps a little."

---

### Complexity Level 3 (Ages 13-17)
Clear, organized symptom description helps your doctor diagnose problems faster and more accurately. Learn to be a good observer of your own body.

**The PQRST Method for Describing Symptoms**:

**P - Provokes/Palliates**
- What triggers or worsens the symptom?
- What provides relief?
- Any patterns you've noticed?

**Q - Quality**
- Describe the character of the symptom
- For pain: sharp, dull, burning, aching, cramping, pressure, stabbing, throbbing
- For other symptoms: constant, intermittent, wave-like, sudden, gradual

**R - Region/Radiation**
- Exactly where is it located?
- Does it spread anywhere?
- Has the location changed?

**S - Severity**
- Rate intensity on 0-10 scale (be consistent)
- How does it compare to other pain you've experienced?
- Does severity change throughout the day?
- How does it affect your daily activities?

**T - Timing**
- When did it first start?
- How long does each episode last?
- How often does it occur?
- Any time-of-day pattern?
- Is it getting better, worse, or staying the same?

**Additional Context**:
- Associated symptoms (nausea, fever, fatigue, etc.)
- What you were doing when it started
- Similar episodes in the past
- Recent changes in your life (diet, activities, stress, sleep)

**Example of Effective Description**:
"I've been having headaches for about two weeks. They're a throbbing pain mostly on the right side of my head, about a 6 out of 10. They usually start in the afternoon and last 2-3 hours. Bright lights make them worse, and lying down in a dark room helps. Sometimes I feel a little nauseous too. They're happening about 4 times a week, which is way more than normal for me."

**Avoid**:
- Minimizing symptoms because you're embarrassed
- Exaggerating to get attention
- Leaving out symptoms that seem unrelated
- Being vague when you have specific information

### Complexity Level 4 (Ages 18+ General Adult)
Effective symptom communication is a skill that improves diagnostic accuracy and treatment outcomes. Your description provides crucial data that tests cannot always reveal.

**Structured Symptom Presentation**:

**Opening Statement**
Lead with a clear, concise chief complaint:
"I'm here because I've been having [main symptom] for [duration]."

**Comprehensive Characterization**:

1. **Onset and Timeline**
   - Precise onset date if possible
   - Sudden vs. gradual onset
   - Progressive, stable, or fluctuating course
   - Any preceding events or exposures

2. **Location and Radiation**
   - Anatomically specific location
   - Diffuse vs. localized
   - Migration or radiation patterns
   - Referred patterns

3. **Quality and Character**
   - Use appropriate medical descriptors
   - Pain: sharp, dull, burning, aching, cramping, colicky, pressure, lancinating
   - Other symptoms: describe precisely what you experience

4. **Severity and Impact**
   - Numeric pain scale (use consistently)
   - Functional limitations
   - Effect on sleep, work, mood, relationships
   - Comparison to previous similar experiences

5. **Temporal Pattern**
   - Constant vs. intermittent
   - Episode duration and frequency
   - Circadian patterns
   - Relationship to meals, activities, menstrual cycle

6. **Modifying Factors**
   - Specific triggers or aggravators
   - Relieving factors
   - Treatments tried and results
   - Medications and dosages used

7. **Associated Symptoms**
   - Related manifestations
   - System-specific symptoms
   - Constitutional symptoms (fever, weight loss, night sweats, fatigue)

**Avoid Common Communication Errors**:
- Minimizing symptoms ("It's probably nothing")
- Using vague terms when specific information is available
- Withholding embarrassing but relevant information
- Presenting symptoms in order of embarrassment rather than clinical importance
- Letting anxiety cause you to forget key details
- Downplaying severity to avoid seeming like a complainer

**When Symptoms Are Difficult to Describe**:
- "It's hard to describe, but it feels like..."
- Use analogies or comparisons
- Describe what it prevents you from doing
- Bring photos, videos, or recordings if relevant

### Complexity Level 5 (Advanced/Medical Professional)
Optimal symptom communication requires structured presentation that aligns with clinical reasoning processes while accurately conveying subjective experience.

**Clinical Communication Framework**:

**Chief Complaint Formulation**
Present a concise statement capturing the primary symptom and duration:
- "Progressive dyspnea on exertion for 3 weeks"
- "Recurrent right lower quadrant pain for 2 months"

**History of Present Illness Structure**:

*Temporal Elements*
- Precise onset characterization (acute, subacute, insidious)
- Duration trajectory (continuous, episodic, paroxysmal)
- Frequency metrics for intermittent symptoms
- Circadian variation and periodicity

*Qualitative Characteristics*
- Utilize standard clinical descriptors:
  - Pain: colicky, pleuritic, lancinating, burning, pressure, aching
  - Dyspnea: at rest, exertional (quantify with activity level)
  - Neurological: paresthesias, numbness, weakness patterns
- Localization with anatomical precision
- Radiation patterns and referred symptoms

*Quantitative Assessment*
- Severity scales appropriate to symptom type
- Functional impact quantification (e.g., "unable to climb one flight of stairs")
- Trend analysis (improving, worsening, plateau)

*Modifying Factors*
- Provocative factors with specificity
- Palliative measures and responses
- Relationship to physiological functions (eating, exercise, sleep, stress)

*Contextual Information*
- Antecedent events or exposures
- Similar prior episodes and their evaluation/treatment
- Recent medical interventions or medication changes
- Relevant psychosocial stressors

**Associated Manifestations**:
- System-specific review targeted to likely differentials
- Red flag symptoms requiring mention
- Negative symptoms pertinent to diagnosis

**Patient-Reported Outcomes**:
- When applicable, reference validated instruments
- Quantify functional status objectively
- Document health-related quality of life impact

**Avoiding Diagnostic Anchoring**:
- Present symptoms objectively without premature diagnosis
- Distinguish symptoms from interpretations
- Note your own theories but let clinician evaluate

## Being Honest with Your Doctor

### Complexity Level 1 (Ages 5-8)
Always tell the doctor the truth! Doctors aren't there to get you in trouble - they just want to help you feel better.

**Things to Be Honest About**:
- How much it really hurts
- What you've been eating
- If you've been sleeping well
- If something scares you
- If you don't understand something

**It's Okay to Tell the Doctor**:
- "I'm scared"
- "That hurts!"
- "I don't want to do that"
- "I don't understand"

**Remember**: Doctors hear all kinds of things from kids. Nothing you say will surprise them or make them mad. Being honest helps them figure out the best way to help you!

**Parents**: Model honesty with healthcare providers. Children learn by watching how you interact with doctors.

---

### Complexity Level 2 (Ages 9-12)
Being honest with your doctor is really important, even when it's embarrassing or hard.

**Why Honesty Matters**:
- Wrong information can lead to wrong treatment
- Some symptoms that embarrass you might be important clues
- Medicines can be dangerous if doctors don't know everything about you
- Being dishonest makes it harder to help you

**Things You Should Always Tell Your Doctor**:
- All the symptoms you're having, even embarrassing ones
- All medicines you're taking, including vitamins
- If you've tried anything to feel better (even if it didn't work)
- If you haven't been taking medicine the way you're supposed to
- If something hurts or doesn't feel right during the exam

**It's Hard But Important to Share**:
- Problems with using the bathroom
- Changes in your body as you grow
- If you've been feeling sad, worried, or stressed
- If something bad happened to you
- If you're being bullied or hurt

**Remember**: Doctors have heard everything before. They won't judge you. Their job is to help, not to get you in trouble.

**Privacy Tip**: You can ask to talk to the doctor alone if there's something you don't want to share in front of others.

---

### Complexity Level 3 (Ages 13-17)
Honest communication with healthcare providers is essential for your safety and health. As a teenager, there are topics that might feel especially difficult to discuss, but honesty is critical.

**Why Honesty Is Non-Negotiable**:
- Inaccurate information leads to inaccurate diagnoses
- Drug interactions can be dangerous or fatal
- Missed information can delay treatment
- Some conditions require truthful disclosure for proper care

**Difficult Topics to Be Honest About**:

*Substances*
- Alcohol, tobacco, vaping
- Marijuana and other drugs
- Misuse of prescription medications
- Supplements, energy drinks, diet pills

*Sexual Health*
- Sexual activity
- Number of partners
- Types of sexual activity
- Birth control use or needs
- Possible pregnancy
- STI concerns

*Mental Health*
- Depression, anxiety, stress
- Self-harm or suicidal thoughts
- Eating concerns
- Sleep problems
- Trauma or abuse

*Lifestyle*
- Diet and exercise habits (honestly)
- Screen time and sleep habits
- Academic and social stress
- Family problems
- Risky behaviors

**Confidentiality Protections**:
- In most states, certain topics are confidential for minors:
  - Sexual health and reproductive care
  - Mental health treatment
  - Substance abuse treatment
  - STI testing and treatment
- Ask your doctor about confidentiality before sharing
- Know what must be reported (abuse, danger to self/others)

**How to Bring Up Difficult Topics**:
- "This is kind of embarrassing, but..."
- "I need to tell you something I haven't told my parents..."
- "Can you explain what stays private before I share?"
- Write it down if saying it is too hard

**What Happens If You're Not Honest**:
- You might get wrong treatment
- Dangerous drug interactions could occur
- Conditions might get worse
- You lose the chance to get help when you need it

---

### Complexity Level 4 (Ages 18+ General Adult)
Candid communication with healthcare providers is fundamental to effective medical care. Withholding information, even from embarrassment or fear of judgment, can compromise your safety and treatment outcomes.

**Critical Information for Full Disclosure**:

**Medications and Substances**
- All prescription medications (including from other providers)
- Over-the-counter medications
- Supplements and herbal products
- Recreational drugs (type, frequency, amount)
- Alcohol consumption (honest about quantity and frequency)
- Tobacco and nicotine products
- Caffeine intake
- CBD or cannabis products

**Sexual and Reproductive Health**
- Sexual activity and practices
- Number and gender of partners
- Contraception use
- Pregnancy status or possibility
- STI history and exposures
- Sexual dysfunction concerns
- History of sexual trauma

**Mental Health**
- Current mental health status
- History of mental health conditions
- Suicidal thoughts or self-harm (past or present)
- Eating disorders
- Trauma history
- Stress levels and sources
- Sleep quality

**Lifestyle Factors**
- Actual diet and exercise habits
- Work and home stress
- Financial concerns affecting health
- Relationship issues
- Safety at home (domestic violence)
- Occupational exposures

**Medical History Honesty**
- Procedures and surgeries (including cosmetic)
- Hospitalizations (including psychiatric)
- Conditions you've been diagnosed with
- Family history (as accurately as you know it)
- Medication adherence (or lack thereof)

**Why Patients Withhold Information**:
- Embarrassment or shame
- Fear of judgment
- Concern about documentation
- Legal worries (illicit drug use)
- Insurance or employment concerns
- Cultural or religious factors
- Fear of family finding out

**Addressing These Concerns**:
- Understand HIPAA protections
- Know that providers are focused on helping, not judging
- Ask about what gets documented and why
- Discuss confidentiality concerns directly
- Remember that providers have seen and heard everything
- Your care depends on complete information

**Consequences of Incomplete Information**:
- Dangerous drug interactions
- Misdiagnosis
- Inappropriate treatment
- Missed preventive opportunities
- Medical emergencies that could have been prevented

---

### Complexity Level 5 (Advanced/Medical Professional)
Complete and accurate patient disclosure is a cornerstone of effective medical care. Understanding the barriers to honest communication and strategies to overcome them supports optimal health outcomes.

**Clinical Significance of Full Disclosure**:

*Diagnostic Accuracy*
- Complete history is essential for differential diagnosis
- Missing information can lead to cognitive errors
- Unexplained symptoms may result from undisclosed factors

*Treatment Safety*
- Drug-drug interactions require complete medication knowledge
- Substance use affects anesthesia and medication dosing
- Pregnancy status is critical for many interventions
- Allergies and adverse reactions must be documented

*Preventive Care*
- Risk assessment requires accurate lifestyle data
- Screening recommendations depend on exposure history
- Family history influences genetic risk assessment

**High-Stakes Disclosure Categories**:

*Substance Use*
- Complete disclosure essential for:
  - Safe anesthesia administration
  - Appropriate pain management
  - Medication selection and dosing
  - Understanding symptom etiology
- Quantification matters (units, frequency, duration)

*Sexual and Reproductive History*
- Critical for appropriate screening
- Affects contraception counseling
- Informs STI testing recommendations
- Impacts pregnancy-related care

*Medication Adherence*
- Non-adherence is common but frequently undisclosed
- Affects interpretation of treatment response
- Guides therapeutic adjustments

*Mental Health and Trauma*
- Influences symptom interpretation
- Affects medication choices
- May explain somatic symptoms
- Impacts patient-provider relationship

**Barriers to Disclosure and Mitigation**:

| Barrier | Mitigation Strategy |
|---------|---------------------|
| Stigma | Non-judgmental interviewing |
| Privacy concerns | Clarify confidentiality protections |
| Fear of documentation | Explain medical record privacy |
| Legal concerns | Discuss mandated reporting limits |
| Time constraints | Prioritize and probe sensitively |
| Language/cultural | Culturally competent care |
| Power differential | Create safe environment |

**Physician Role in Promoting Honesty**:
- Establish rapport before sensitive questioning
- Normalize difficult topics ("I ask everyone about...")
- Use non-judgmental language
- Provide explicit confidentiality assurance where applicable
- Create opportunities for disclosure ("Anything else?")
- Accept and respond constructively to disclosures

## Understanding Instructions

### Complexity Level 1 (Ages 5-8)
The doctor will tell you what to do to feel better. Here's how to remember:

**Listen Carefully**:
- Look at the doctor when they talk
- Don't play with toys when they're giving instructions
- Nod your head to show you understand

**Ask If You Don't Understand**:
- "Can you say that again?"
- "What does that word mean?"
- "Can you show me?"

**The Grown-Up Will Help**:
- Your parent will write things down
- They'll remember for you
- They can explain later in words you understand better

**Getting Medicine Instructions**:
- How much to take (maybe a spoonful or a dropper full)
- When to take it (morning, night, or both)
- How to take it (with food, with water)
- What it should taste like or look like

**Remembering What to Do**:
- Have the grown-up make a chart
- Put stickers on a calendar when you take medicine
- Set a fun alarm as a reminder

---

### Complexity Level 2 (Ages 9-12)
Understanding what the doctor tells you is just as important as going to the appointment. Here's how to make sure you get it right.

**While the Doctor Is Talking**:
- Really listen - don't just wait for your turn to talk
- Write down important things (or have a parent write)
- Watch if they show you something (like how to use an inhaler)

**What to Make Sure You Understand**:

About Medicine:
- Name of the medicine
- How much to take (dose)
- How often (twice a day? every 8 hours?)
- When to take it (with food? before bed?)
- How long to take it (7 days? until it's all gone?)
- What if you miss a dose?
- Side effects to watch for

About Follow-Up:
- When to come back
- What symptoms mean you should call sooner
- How to get test results
- Who to call with questions

**Teach-Back Method**:
Repeat the instructions back to make sure you got them right:
- "So I take this pill twice a day with food for 10 days, right?"
- "And I should call if the fever comes back?"

**If You Don't Understand**:
- Say "I don't understand, can you explain differently?"
- Ask them to write it down
- Ask if there are pictures or videos
- Ask for simpler words

**Getting Written Instructions**:
Many offices give you a printed summary. This includes:
- Your diagnosis
- Your medicines and how to take them
- Follow-up instructions
- Warning signs to watch for
- Contact information

---

### Complexity Level 3 (Ages 13-17)
Understanding medical instructions is a skill you need to develop as you become more independent with your healthcare. Misunderstanding can lead to ineffective treatment or even harm.

**Active Listening Strategies**:
- Put away your phone and focus
- Take notes on key points
- Ask questions as they come up
- Don't pretend to understand when you don't

**Core Information to Capture**:

**Diagnosis**
- Name of condition (get spelling)
- What causes it
- What to expect over time
- Potential complications

**Treatment Plan**
- All prescribed medications with complete instructions
- Non-medication treatments (rest, ice, exercises)
- Lifestyle modifications
- What to avoid

**Medication Specifics**
- Name (brand and generic)
- Purpose (what it treats)
- Dose and frequency
- Duration of treatment
- How to take (with food, before bed, etc.)
- Side effects to expect
- Side effects requiring medical attention
- What happens if you miss a dose
- Interactions to avoid (foods, other medicines)

**The Teach-Back Technique**:
Research shows repeating instructions back improves understanding and adherence.
- "Let me make sure I understand..."
- "So what you're saying is..."
- "I'm going to repeat this back to make sure I have it right..."

**Questions to Ask**:
- "What's the most important thing I should remember?"
- "What are the warning signs that something is wrong?"
- "What should I do if [specific situation]?"
- "Is there written information I can take home?"
- "Can you show me how to do that?"

**Resources for Understanding**:
- After-visit summaries from the patient portal
- Pharmacy printouts with medication information
- Reputable websites your doctor recommends
- Follow-up call to the office if questions arise

**Red Flags You Didn't Understand**:
- You can't explain the plan to someone else
- You're not sure what to do when you get home
- The instructions seem unclear or contradictory
- You're guessing about what to do

---

### Complexity Level 4 (Ages 18+ General Adult)
Comprehending and retaining medical instructions directly impacts health outcomes. Studies show patients forget 40-80% of medical information immediately after a visit. Use strategies to maximize understanding and retention.

**Components of Complete Understanding**:

**Diagnosis Comprehension**
- Medical term and plain-language explanation
- Underlying cause or mechanism
- Prognosis and expected course
- Relationship to other health conditions
- Implications for daily life

**Treatment Plan Elements**
- All therapeutic interventions recommended
- Rationale for each component
- Expected timeline for improvement
- Criteria for treatment success or failure
- Plan if initial treatment fails

**Medication Instructions**
For each prescribed medication:
- Name (document both brand and generic)
- Indication (what it's treating)
- Mechanism (how it works, if helpful to know)
- Dosage and frequency
- Route of administration
- Duration of therapy
- Administration requirements (food, timing, positioning)
- Expected benefits and timeline
- Common side effects
- Serious adverse effects requiring immediate attention
- Missed dose protocol
- Drug, food, and alcohol interactions
- Storage requirements
- Refill information

**Self-Care Instructions**
- Activity modifications or restrictions
- Dietary changes
- Symptom management at home
- Wound or device care
- Exercises or physical therapy

**Monitoring and Follow-Up**
- Symptoms requiring urgent contact
- Symptoms requiring emergency care
- Scheduled follow-up appointments
- Test or procedure scheduling
- How to obtain test results
- Who to contact for different issues

**Strategies for Understanding and Retention**:

*During the Visit*
- Take written notes or use a voice recorder (with permission)
- Use teach-back: "Let me confirm I understand correctly..."
- Ask for clarification immediately when confused
- Request written instructions
- Ask about visual aids or demonstrations
- Bring a companion to help remember

*After the Visit*
- Review written instructions immediately
- Use the patient portal for after-visit summaries
- Call with questions while information is fresh
- Set up medication reminders
- Create a clear system for following the plan
- Share instructions with family members or caregivers

**Questions to Ensure Understanding**:
- "What is the most important thing for me to do?"
- "What could go wrong, and what should I do if it does?"
- "Is there anything that should make me call you or go to the ER?"
- "How will I know if the treatment is working?"
- "What do I do if I have questions later?"

---

### Complexity Level 5 (Advanced/Medical Professional)
Health literacy research demonstrates significant gaps between information provided and patient comprehension. Effective instruction understanding requires systematic approaches to information processing and retention.

**Cognitive Load Considerations**:
- Medical visits create high cognitive demand
- Anxiety impairs information processing
- Novel or alarming information may overwhelm working memory
- Multiple instructions compete for attention

**Systematic Instruction Capture**:

**Structured Documentation Protocol**
- Diagnosis with full nomenclature
- Differential diagnoses considered
- Diagnostic reasoning rationale
- Complete treatment plan with alternatives discussed
- Contingency planning for treatment failure

**Medication Documentation Standard**
- Proprietary and non-proprietary names
- Therapeutic class and mechanism
- Specific indication in clinical context
- Complete dosing regimen with duration
- Administration requirements and technique
- Expected therapeutic response and timeline
- Monitoring requirements
- Adverse effect profile (common, serious, idiosyncratic)
- Drug-drug, drug-food, drug-disease interactions
- Contraindications relevant to patient
- Discontinuation considerations

**Ensuring Comprehension**:

*Teach-Back Method (Evidence-Based)*
Research supports teach-back as effective for confirming understanding:
- Patient restates instructions in own words
- Clinician clarifies misunderstandings
- Process repeats until accurate understanding confirmed

*Chunk and Check*
- Information presented in small segments
- Understanding verified before proceeding
- Complex instructions broken into manageable components

*Dual Verification*
- Verbal instructions confirmed with written materials
- Key points highlighted and reviewed
- Patient identifies most important elements

**Health Literacy Accommodation**:
- Assess health literacy without causing embarrassment
- Adjust communication to patient's level
- Use plain language without being condescending
- Provide written materials at appropriate reading level
- Include visual aids and diagrams
- Offer video instruction for complex procedures

**Post-Visit Comprehension Support**:
- After-visit summary with complete instructions
- Patient portal access to notes and results
- Structured follow-up for complex plans
- Care coordinator involvement for multi-component plans
- Printed medication lists with full instructions

## Asking for Clarification

### Complexity Level 1 (Ages 5-8)
If you don't understand something the doctor says, it's really important to ask! Here are some ways to ask:

- "What does that mean?"
- "Can you say it in easier words?"
- "Can you show me?"
- "I don't get it"
- "Huh?" (It's okay to say this!)

**Remember**: Doctors use big words sometimes, and it's totally normal not to understand them. Asking questions is smart, not silly!

The doctor might:
- Use different words
- Draw a picture
- Show you on a model or doll
- Explain it to your parent so they can help you understand

**Parent Tip**: Model asking for clarification yourself. Say things like, "Doctor, can you explain that in simpler terms so we all understand?"

---

### Complexity Level 2 (Ages 9-12)
Asking questions when you don't understand isn't a sign of being dumb - it's actually really smart! Doctors use complicated words, and it's totally normal to need things explained differently.

**Good Ways to Ask for Clarification**:
- "Can you explain what that word means?"
- "I'm not sure I understand - can you say it another way?"
- "So what you're saying is...?" (then see if you got it right)
- "What exactly should I do when I get home?"
- "Can you write that down for me?"

**It's Especially Important to Ask When**:
- You hear a word you don't know
- The instructions seem confusing
- You're not sure why you need a certain treatment
- You don't understand how to take medicine
- You're confused about what to do next

**Don't Be Afraid to**:
- Ask the same question more than once
- Ask for simpler words
- Ask for examples
- Ask to see a picture or diagram
- Ask for something in writing

**If You're Too Shy to Ask**:
- Write your questions down and hand them to the doctor
- Ask a parent to ask for you
- Send a message through the patient portal after the visit
- Call the office later with your question

**Remember**: The doctor would rather take extra time to explain than have you misunderstand and do something wrong.

---

### Complexity Level 3 (Ages 13-17)
Asking for clarification is a crucial communication skill. Medical information can be complex, and misunderstanding can have real consequences for your health.

**Why Clarification Matters**:
- Prevents medication errors
- Ensures you can follow the treatment plan
- Helps you understand your health condition
- Makes you an active participant in your care

**Effective Clarification Requests**:

*When You Don't Know a Word*
- "What does [term] mean?"
- "I'm not familiar with that word - can you explain?"
- "Is there a common name for that?"

*When the Explanation Is Confusing*
- "I'm having trouble following - can you break that down?"
- "Can you explain that in a different way?"
- "Can you give me an example?"
- "What does that mean in practical terms?"

*When You Need Specifics*
- "What exactly should I do when...?"
- "What are the specific steps?"
- "How will I know if it's working?"
- "What should I watch out for?"

*When You're Not Sure You Got It*
- "Let me repeat that back to make sure I understand..."
- "So you're saying that I should..."
- "Can I summarize what I heard to make sure I got it right?"

**Written and Visual Clarification**:
- Ask for written instructions
- Request diagrams or pictures
- Ask about patient education materials
- Ask if there are videos you can watch

**After the Visit**:
- Read through written materials and call with questions
- Use the patient portal to send questions
- Research your condition on recommended websites
- Write down questions for your next visit

**Don't Feel Embarrassed**:
- Healthcare providers prefer questions to misunderstandings
- Asking shows you're engaged and taking responsibility
- There's no such thing as a stupid question about your health

---

### Complexity Level 4 (Ages 18+ General Adult)
Seeking clarification is a sign of engaged, responsible healthcare participation. Research shows that patients who ask questions have better health outcomes.

**Common Barriers to Asking for Clarification**:
- Embarrassment about not understanding
- Fear of appearing unintelligent
- Feeling rushed during appointments
- Power differential with providers
- Assumption that it will become clearer later
- Belief that asking takes too much time

**Overcoming These Barriers**:
- Remember that understanding is essential for safe care
- Recognize that medical terminology is a specialized language
- Know that providers want you to understand
- Accept that clarification is your right as a patient
- Understand that a few minutes now prevents problems later

**Effective Clarification Techniques**:

*For Unfamiliar Terms*
- "I'm not familiar with that term - what does it mean?"
- "Can you explain that in plain language?"
- "What is the common name for that?"
- "How would you describe that to a friend?"

*For Complex Explanations*
- "That's a lot to take in - can we go through it step by step?"
- "Can you explain the most important part first?"
- "What's the bottom line here?"
- "Can you give me a concrete example?"

*For Confirmation*
- "Let me make sure I understand correctly..."
- "So what I need to do is..."
- "To summarize..."
- "I want to repeat this back to make sure I have it right."

*For Written Information*
- "Can you write that down for me?"
- "Do you have printed information I can take home?"
- "Can you draw a diagram or show me a picture?"
- "Is there a website you recommend for more information?"

**When to Seek Clarification**:
- Any term you don't understand
- Instructions that seem unclear or complicated
- Side effects or warning signs to watch for
- Anything that doesn't make sense to you
- Information that seems contradictory

**Post-Visit Clarification Options**:
- Call the office with questions
- Send a message through the patient portal
- Schedule a phone or telehealth follow-up
- Ask the pharmacist to explain medication information
- Request a nurse callback

### Complexity Level 5 (Advanced/Medical Professional)
Clarification-seeking is a core patient engagement behavior that improves safety and outcomes. Effective clarification requires specific communication skills and strategies.

**Communication Science Foundation**:

*Why Clarification Fails*
- Patients often don't recognize when they don't understand
- Social desirability leads to false confirmation of understanding
- Medical complexity exceeds health literacy levels
- Time pressure discourages thorough clarification
- Power dynamics inhibit questioning

*Effective Clarification Behaviors*
- Active uncertainty expression
- Specific questioning rather than general uncertainty
- Verification of understanding through restatement
- Persistence until actual comprehension achieved

**Clarification Request Taxonomy**:

1. **Lexical Clarification**
   - Request definition of unfamiliar terms
   - Seek plain language equivalents
   - Ask about acronyms and abbreviations

2. **Conceptual Clarification**
   - Request explanation of mechanisms or processes
   - Seek analogies or comparisons
   - Ask for visual representations

3. **Procedural Clarification**
   - Request step-by-step instructions
   - Seek demonstrations
   - Ask for practice opportunities (teach-back)

4. **Applicability Clarification**
   - Ask how general information applies specifically to you
   - Seek examples relevant to your situation
   - Request practical implementation guidance

5. **Verification Clarification**
   - Restate understanding for confirmation
   - Summarize key points
   - Identify areas of remaining uncertainty

**Strategic Clarification Approaches**:

*Pre-Visit Preparation*
- Research condition and treatment basics
- Prepare specific questions
- Identify areas of uncertainty in advance

*During-Visit Strategies*
- State explicitly when you don't understand
- Request explanations at your level
- Use teach-back proactively
- Take notes for later review
- Ask for written materials

*Post-Visit Follow-Up*
- Review materials and identify remaining questions
- Utilize patient portal for non-urgent clarification
- Contact office for urgent concerns
- Seek additional resources for complex topics

## Taking Notes

### Complexity Level 1 (Ages 5-8)
The grown-up with you will write down what the doctor says. But you can help too!

**After the Visit, You Can Remember By**:
- Drawing a picture of what the doctor told you
- Telling someone else what happened at the doctor
- Putting a fun sticker on a chart to remember your medicine times

**Your Grown-Up Might**:
- Write things in a notebook
- Type on their phone
- Ask the doctor for a paper to take home
- Take a picture of important instructions

**Why It's Important to Remember**:
- So you know how to feel better
- So you take medicine at the right time
- So you know when to come back

### Complexity Level 2 (Ages 9-12)
Taking notes during your doctor's visit helps you remember important information. Here's how to do it well.

**What to Write Down**:
- Your diagnosis (what's wrong)
- Medicines you need to take
- When and how to take them
- When to come back
- Warning signs to watch for
- Phone numbers if you have questions

**Tips for Good Note-Taking**:
- Bring a small notebook or use your phone
- Write the date at the top
- Don't try to write everything - just the important stuff
- Ask the doctor to slow down if they're talking too fast
- Use abbreviations: 2x/day = twice a day, w/ food = with food

**After the Visit**:
- Look over your notes while they're fresh
- Ask questions if something doesn't make sense
- Put your notes somewhere you'll remember them
- Take a picture of any handouts

**If Taking Notes Is Hard**:
- Ask if you can record the visit on your phone
- Bring someone to write for you
- Ask for written instructions
- Most offices give you a printed summary

**Example Notes**:
```
Date: Oct 15
Dr. Smith visit - sore throat

Diagnosis: Strep throat (streptococcal pharyngitis)
Medicine: Amoxicillin 500mg
- Take 2x per day (morning + night) with food
- Take ALL 10 days, even when I feel better!
Side effects: upset stomach, diarrhea possible
Call if: fever returns, can't swallow, rash
Follow-up: only if not better in 3-4 days
```

**Essential Information to Capture**:

### Complexity Level 4 (Ages 18+ General Adult)
Effective note-taking during medical visits improves information retention and adherence to treatment plans. Given the amount of information exchanged, documentation is essential.

**Preparation for Note-Taking**:

- Charge devices if recording or typing

**Structured Note Framework**:

**Visit Information**

**Assessment**

### Complexity Level 5 (Advanced/Medical Professional)
Comprehensive visit documentation serves multiple functions: ensures accurate treatment implementation, creates personal health record, and facilitates care coordination.

**Documentation Framework**:

**Structured Clinical Note Template**

*Header Information*
- Date, time, location, provider, specialty
- Visit type (new, follow-up, urgent, telemedicine)
- Chief complaint/reason for visit

*History of Present Illness Discussion*
- Key elements discussed
- Clinical reasoning shared
- Differential diagnoses considered

*Examination Findings*
- Relevant physical findings shared
- Abnormalities noted
- Baseline measurements documented

*Assessment*
- Working diagnosis with confidence level
- ICD codes if provided
- Staging/severity if applicable
- Prognosis discussed

*Plan*
- Diagnostic studies ordered
- Therapeutic interventions prescribed
- Procedural planning
- Referrals initiated
- Follow-up arrangements

*Medications*
For each:
- Name, dose, frequency, route, duration
- Indication
- Titration schedule if applicable
- Monitoring requirements
- Interactions to avoid

*Patient Education Provided*
- Key teaching points
- Materials provided
- Resources recommended
- Questions answered

*Action Items*
- Patient responsibilities
- Pending items requiring follow-up
- Scheduled appointments
- Contact triggers

**Documentation Methods**:

*Real-Time Documentation*
- Structured templates on mobile device
- Key point capture during discussion
- Request permission for audio recording
- Request copies of clinician notes

*Post-Visit Documentation*
- Supplement with after-visit summary
- Compare personal notes to official summary
- Identify discrepancies for clarification
- Request medical records for complete documentation

**Integration with Personal Health Record**:
- Consolidate notes from all providers
- Maintain chronological and problem-based organization
- Link to supporting documents (test results, imaging)
- Update medication list after each visit
- Track pending items and follow-up
