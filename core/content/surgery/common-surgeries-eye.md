# Common Surgeries: Cataract Surgery

## Overview

Cataract surgery is the most commonly performed surgery in the United States, with over 4 million procedures annually. It is also one of the safest and most successful surgeries, with over 95% of patients experiencing improved vision.

**Complexity Level: 2 (Basic)**

---

## Understanding Cataracts

### What Is a Cataract?

A cataract is a clouding of the eye's natural lens. The lens, located behind the pupil, focuses light onto the retina to create clear images. When the lens becomes cloudy, vision becomes blurred, like looking through a foggy window.

### Normal Eye vs. Cataract

**Healthy Lens**
- Clear and transparent
- Focuses light precisely on retina
- Provides sharp, colorful vision

**Cataractous Lens**
- Cloudy and opaque
- Scatters light
- Causes blurry, dim, or faded vision

### Who Gets Cataracts?

**Age is the Primary Factor**
- Age 40-54: ~5% have cataracts
- Age 55-64: ~20% have cataracts
- Age 65-74: ~40% have cataracts
- Age 75+: ~70%+ have cataracts

**Risk Factors**
- Aging (most significant)
- Diabetes
- Smoking
- Excessive sunlight exposure
- Previous eye injury or surgery
- Prolonged steroid use
- Family history

### Symptoms of Cataracts

**Visual Changes**
- Blurry or cloudy vision
- Colors appear faded or yellowed
- Difficulty with night driving (glare from headlights)
- Halos around lights
- Frequent prescription changes
- Need for brighter light for reading
- Double vision in one eye

**Progression**
- Cataracts usually develop slowly over years
- Both eyes often affected (may differ in severity)
- Eventually interfere with daily activities

## When Is Surgery Recommended?

### The Decision to Operate

Cataract surgery is an elective procedure recommended when:
- Vision impairs daily activities (reading, driving, working)
- Difficulty with hobbies or quality of life
- Night driving becomes unsafe
- Glasses no longer adequately improve vision
- Cataracts interfere with treatment of other eye conditions

### Surgery Timing

**You Choose When**
- No medical urgency in most cases
- Surgery recommended when cataracts affect your life
- Waiting doesn't make surgery more difficult
- Don't wait until vision is severely impaired

**Reasons Not to Delay Too Long**
- Dense cataracts can be slightly more complex to remove
- Continued vision loss affects quality of life
- May become unable to drive safely

## Types of Cataract Surgery

### Phacoemulsification (Standard Modern Technique)

The most common approach, often called "phaco":
- Small incision (2-3 mm)
- Ultrasound probe breaks up cloudy lens
- Lens fragments suctioned out
- Artificial lens implanted
- Usually no stitches needed
- Quick recovery

### Laser-Assisted Cataract Surgery

Uses femtosecond laser for some steps:
- Laser makes incision
- Laser softens lens
- Remainder of surgery similar to phaco
- May improve precision
- Additional cost (often not covered by insurance)
- Similar outcomes to phaco for most patients

### Extracapsular Cataract Extraction (ECCE)

Traditional technique for very dense cataracts:
- Larger incision
- Lens removed in one piece
- Requires stitches
- Longer recovery
- Rarely needed today

## Understanding Lens Implants (IOLs)

### What Is an Intraocular Lens?

During cataract surgery, the cloudy natural lens is replaced with an artificial lens called an intraocular lens (IOL). This permanent implant focuses light on the retina.

### Types of IOLs

**Monofocal IOLs (Standard)**
- One focusing distance
- Most commonly set for distance vision
- Reading glasses needed after surgery
- Covered by insurance

**Multifocal IOLs**
- Multiple focusing distances
- Reduce need for glasses
- Some patients notice halos or glare
- Additional cost (premium lens)

**Accommodating IOLs**
- Move slightly to change focus
- More natural focusing
- Additional cost

**Toric IOLs**
- Correct astigmatism
- Reduce dependence on glasses
- Additional cost for astigmatism correction

### Choosing Your Lens

**Factors to Consider**
- Your visual goals and lifestyle
- Current glasses prescription
- Presence of astigmatism
- Other eye conditions
- Budget and insurance coverage

**Discussion with Your Surgeon**
- What activities are most important to you?
- How do you feel about wearing glasses after surgery?
- Do you drive at night frequently?
- What is your budget for lens options?

## Before Cataract Surgery

### Preoperative Evaluation

**Comprehensive Eye Exam**
- Vision testing
- Eye pressure measurement
- Dilated exam of lens and retina
- Assessment of other eye conditions

**Measurements for Lens Selection**
- Eye length (axial length)
- Corneal curvature
- These precise measurements determine IOL power

**Medical Clearance**
- Usually not required for healthy patients
- May need clearance if significant medical conditions

### Preparing for Surgery

**Medications**
- Review all medications with surgeon
- Blood thinners: Usually can continue (confirm with surgeon)
- Most regular medications can continue
- Antibiotic eye drops may be prescribed to start before surgery

**Day Before Surgery**
- Fill prescriptions for post-op eye drops
- Arrange transportation (you cannot drive)
- Wash face and don't wear makeup day of surgery
- No food or drink after midnight (or as instructed)

---

## Day of Surgery

### What to Expect

**Arrival**
- Arrive 1-2 hours before surgery
- Sign consent forms
- Pupil-dilating drops given
- IV may be placed for sedation
- Numbing eye drops applied

### The Procedure

**Duration**: 15-30 minutes per eye

**Anesthesia**
- Topical: Numbing drops only (most common)
- Local: Injection near eye (occasionally used)
- IV sedation: Makes you relaxed but awake
- General anesthesia: Rarely needed

**What Happens**
1. Eye numbed and pupil dilated
2. Tiny incision made in cornea
3. Circular opening made in lens capsule
4. Ultrasound probe breaks up cloudy lens
5. Lens fragments removed by suction
6. Artificial lens implanted through small incision
7. Lens unfolds and positions itself
8. Incision self-seals (usually no stitches)

### What You'll Experience

- You'll be awake but very relaxed
- No pain during the procedure
- May see lights and movement
- Surgeon and team will talk to you
- Over before you know it

### Immediately After

**Recovery Area**
- Observation for 15-30 minutes
- Eye shield placed
- Instructions reviewed
- Discharge home same day

## After Cataract Surgery

### Day of Surgery and First Night

**Vision**
- Blurry immediately after
- May have scratchy or gritty feeling
- Light sensitivity normal
- Some tearing normal

**Activity**
- Go home and rest
- Avoid bending over, lifting
- Sleep with eye shield
- Take it easy

### First Week

**Vision Changes**
- Improvement often noticeable within days
- Vision continues to improve over 1-4 weeks
- Some fluctuation normal

**Eye Drops (Critical)**

Typical regimen (follow your specific instructions):
- Antibiotic drops: Prevents infection
- Steroid drops: Reduces inflammation
- NSAID drops: Sometimes prescribed

**Drop schedule example:**
- Start drops day after surgery
- Multiple times daily, gradually tapering
- Continue for 4-6 weeks
- Proper technique matters

**How to Apply Eye Drops**
1. Wash hands thoroughly
2. Tilt head back or lie down
3. Pull down lower eyelid to create pocket
4. Drop medication into pocket (don't touch eye)
5. Close eye gently for 1-2 minutes
6. Wait 5 minutes between different drops

### Restrictions (First Week)

**Avoid**
- Rubbing or pressing on eye
- Getting water, soap, or shampoo in eye
- Bending at waist (bend at knees instead)
- Heavy lifting (over 10-20 pounds)
- Strenuous exercise
- Swimming, hot tubs
- Dusty or dirty environments

**Can Usually Do**
- Watch TV, read (rest eyes if fatigued)
- Light walking
- Normal eating
- Showering (keep eye closed, avoid direct water)

### First Month

**Follow-Up Appointments**
- Day after surgery
- 1 week after surgery
- 3-4 weeks after surgery

**Gradual Return to Normal**
- Driving when vision adequate (often within days)
- Light exercise after 1-2 weeks
- Swimming after 2-4 weeks
- Normal activities by 4 weeks

### New Glasses

**Timing**
- Wait 4-6 weeks for final prescription
- Vision must stabilize first
- Will likely need reading glasses (with standard IOL)
- May need updated distance glasses

## Second Eye Surgery

### Timing Between Eyes

If both eyes have cataracts:
- Typically wait 1-4 weeks between surgeries
- Allows first eye to heal
- Can assess results before second surgery
- Some patients do both closer together

### Benefits of Doing Both

- Balanced vision
- Better depth perception
- Maximum visual improvement

---

## Potential Complications

### Rare but Possible

**During Surgery**
- Posterior capsule tear
- Lens fragments falling into eye
- May require additional treatment

**After Surgery**
- **Infection (endophthalmitis)**: Very rare (<0.1%), but serious
  - Call immediately if: increasing pain, redness, decreased vision
- **Increased eye pressure**: Usually temporary, treated with drops
- **Swelling of macula (CME)**: Can occur weeks later, treatable
- **Retinal detachment**: Rare, requires urgent treatment
- **Droopy eyelid**: Usually temporary
- **Dry eye**: Common, usually improves

### Posterior Capsule Opacification (PCO)

**What It Is**
- Clouding of membrane behind lens implant
- Occurs in 20-30% of patients
- Can happen months to years after surgery
- Sometimes called "secondary cataract"

**Symptoms**
- Gradual return of blurry vision
- Similar to original cataract symptoms

**Treatment**
- YAG laser capsulotomy
- Painless 5-minute procedure
- Immediate improvement
- Permanent solution

## Expected Outcomes

### Vision Results

**Success Rate**
- Over 95% have improved vision
- Most achieve driving-level vision without glasses
- Results depend on overall eye health

**Factors Affecting Outcome**
- Presence of other eye conditions (macular degeneration, glaucoma)
- Accuracy of lens power calculation
- Healing response
- Type of lens implant chosen

### Adaptation Period

**What to Expect**
- Colors appear brighter (removing yellow lens)
- May notice more blue tones
- Brain adapts over days to weeks
- Floaters may become more noticeable (were always there)

---

## Special Situations

### Diabetes and Cataracts

- Cataracts may develop earlier
- Blood sugar control important for healing
- May have other eye conditions affecting results
- Require more frequent follow-up

### Cataracts After Other Eye Conditions

- Glaucoma: Surgery still possible, may need adjustments
- Macular degeneration: Cataracts can be removed, but vision limited by macula
- Previous retinal surgery: May affect lens calculations

### Cataracts in Children (Congenital)

- Different cause than age-related cataracts
- Surgery approach different
- Important for visual development

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is cataract surgery painful?**
No. The eye is completely numbed. Most patients feel nothing during the procedure.

**Will I be awake during surgery?**
Yes, but you'll be sedated and relaxed. The procedure is quick and comfortable.

**How soon can I drive after surgery?**
Many patients can drive within a few days, once vision is clear. Follow your doctor's advice.

**Why do I need drops for weeks?**
Drops prevent infection and reduce inflammation during healing. They're essential for good outcomes.

**Can cataracts come back?**
No. Once removed, the cataract cannot return. However, the membrane behind the lens can become cloudy (PCO), which is easily treated with laser.

**Will I still need glasses?**
With standard monofocal lenses, most people need reading glasses. Some premium lenses reduce this need.

**What if I have cataracts in both eyes?**
Surgery is done one eye at a time, usually weeks apart.
