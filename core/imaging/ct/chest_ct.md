## Introduction

### What is a Chest CT?

Chest CT uses X-rays and computer processing to create detailed cross-sectional images of the lungs, heart, mediastinum, and chest wall. It provides far more detail than chest X-ray and is essential for diagnosing many thoracic conditions.

### Indications

**Emergency Indications:**
- Suspected pulmonary embolism (PE)
- Aortic dissection
- Chest trauma
- Hemoptysis evaluation
- Severe pneumonia not responding to treatment

**Elective Indications:**
- Lung nodule characterization/follow-up
- Lung cancer staging
- Interstitial lung disease evaluation
- Mediastinal mass workup
- Preoperative planning
- Lung cancer screening (high-risk patients)

### Radiation Dose

| Study | Effective Dose (mSv) |
|-------|---------------------|
| Chest X-ray | 0.02 |
| Low-dose CT (screening) | 1-2 |
| Standard Chest CT | 7 |
| CT Angiography | 10-15 |

## How Chest CT Works

### Patient-Friendly Explanation

"A chest CT takes detailed pictures of your lungs, heart, and the surrounding structures. You'll lie on a table that moves through a doughnut-shaped scanner. The scan takes just a few seconds and you may need to hold your breath briefly. Some scans require contrast dye injected into your vein to highlight blood vessels."

### Technical Principles

**Hounsfield Units (HU):**
- Air: -1000 HU (black)
- Fat: -100 HU
- Water: 0 HU
- Soft tissue: 40-80 HU
- Bone: +1000 HU (white)

**Contrast Enhancement:**
- Iodinated contrast for vascular studies
- Timing determines what is opacified
- Arterial phase: Pulmonary arteries (PE protocol)
- Mixed phase: Aorta and pulmonary vessels

## Protocols and Techniques

### Non-Contrast Chest CT

**Indications:**
- Lung nodule characterization
- Interstitial lung disease
- Emphysema assessment
- Lung cancer screening

**Technique:**
- Inspiration breath-hold
- 1-2mm slice thickness for high resolution
- Soft tissue and lung windows

### CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA)

**Indications:**
- Suspected pulmonary embolism

**Technique:**
- IV contrast bolus
- Bolus tracking in pulmonary artery
- Scan when pulmonary arteries opacify

**Quality Assessment:**
- Good opacification of pulmonary arteries
- Minimal motion artifact
- Adequate coverage

### High-Resolution CT (HRCT)

**Indications:**
- Interstitial lung disease
- Bronchiectasis
- Airways disease

**Technique:**
- Thin sections (1-1.25mm)
- Inspiration AND expiration images
- Prone imaging (for posterior lung assessment)
- Lung algorithm reconstruction

### CT Aortography

**Indications:**
- Suspected aortic dissection
- Aortic aneurysm
- Trauma (aortic injury)

**Technique:**
- Non-contrast (for IMH, calcification)
- Arterial phase with contrast
- ECG gating for root/ascending aorta

### Window Settings

| Window | Width (W) | Level (L) | Use |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-----|
| Lung | 1500 | -600 | Lung parenchyma |
| Mediastinum | 400 | 40 | Soft tissue, mediastinum |
| Bone | 2000 | 400 | Ribs, spine |
| PE | 700 | 100 | Clot visualization |

## Normal Anatomy

### Lung Anatomy

**Lobes:**
- Right: Upper, Middle, Lower (3 lobes)
- Left: Upper, Lower (2 lobes)
- Lingula is part of left upper lobe

**Fissures:**
- Oblique (major): Separates upper/middle from lower lobes
- Horizontal (minor): Right only, separates upper from middle

**Bronchopulmonary Segments:**
- Right: 10 segments
- Left: 8-10 segments
- Each has its own bronchus and artery

### Mediastinal Compartments

**Anterior:**
- Thymus
- Internal mammary vessels
- Lymph nodes
- Fat

**Middle:**
- Heart
- Pericardium
- Ascending aorta
- SVC/IVC
- Main pulmonary artery
- Trachea
- Main bronchi

**Posterior:**
- Descending aorta
- Esophagus
- Azygos/hemiazygos veins
- Thoracic duct
- Sympathetic chains

### Lymph Node Stations

**Supraclavicular:** Station 1
**Upper Paratracheal:** Stations 2R, 2L
**Prevascular/Retrotracheal:** Stations 3a, 3p
**Lower Paratracheal:** Stations 4R, 4L
**Subaortic (AP Window):** Station 5
**Para-aortic:** Station 6
**Subcarinal:** Station 7
**Paraesophageal:** Station 8
**Pulmonary Ligament:** Station 9
**Hilar:** Stations 10R, 10L
**Interlobar:** Station 11
**Lobar:** Station 12

## Pulmonary Embolism

### CT Findings

**Direct Signs:**
- Filling defect in pulmonary artery
- Complete occlusion (no contrast beyond)
- Partial filling defect (contrast around clot)
- "Railway track" sign (clot outlined by contrast)

**Indirect Signs:**
- Wedge-shaped peripheral infarct
- Pleural effusion
- Atelectasis
- RV enlargement (RV:LV ratio >1)
- Reflux of contrast into IVC

### Location Classification

**Central (Main/Lobar):**
- Main pulmonary artery
- Right or left pulmonary artery
- Lobar arteries

**Segmental:**
- Segmental arteries

**Subsegmental:**
- Smaller peripheral arteries
- Clinical significance debated

### Severity Assessment

**CT Findings of RV Strain:**
- RV:LV diameter ratio >1.0
- Interventricular septal bowing toward LV
- Contrast reflux into IVC and hepatic veins
- SVC distension

**Clot Burden Scoring:**
- Various scoring systems exist
- Estimate percentage of vascular tree affected

### Pitfalls

**False Positives:**
- Motion artifact
- Flow artifact (incomplete mixing)
- Lymph nodes adjacent to vessels
- Mucous plugging (mimics in airways)

**False Negatives:**
- Poor contrast opacification
- Motion artifact
- Subsegmental clots (small)

## Lung Nodules and Masses

### Definitions

- **Nodule:** <3 cm
- **Mass:** â‰¥3 cm (always suspicious)
- **Ground-glass nodule:** Does not obscure vessels
- **Part-solid nodule:** Has both ground-glass and solid components
- **Solid nodule:** Completely soft tissue density

### Nodule Characterization

**Size:**
- <6mm: Very low risk
- 6-8mm: Low-intermediate risk
- >8mm: Intermediate-high risk

**Margins:**
- Smooth: Usually benign
- Lobulated: Intermediate
- Spiculated/corona radiata: Suspicious for malignancy

**Internal Features:**
- Calcification patterns
- Fat (-40 to -120 HU): Hamartoma
- Cavitation

### Calcification Patterns

| Pattern | Significance |
|---------|--------------|
| Central | Benign (granuloma) |
| Diffuse solid | Benign (granuloma) |
| Laminated/concentric | Benign (granuloma) |
| Popcorn | Benign (hamartoma) |
| Eccentric | Suspicious |
| Stippled | Suspicious |

### Lung-RADS Classification

For lung cancer screening:

| Category | Finding | Management |
|----------|---------|------------|
| 1 | Negative | Continue annual screening |
| 2 | Benign | Continue annual screening |
| 3 | Probably benign | 6-month follow-up |
| 4A | Suspicious | 3-month follow-up or PET |
| 4B | Very suspicious | PET and/or tissue sampling |
| 4X | Very suspicious + additional features | PET and/or tissue sampling |

### Growth Assessment

**Volume Doubling Time:**
- <400 days: Suspicious for malignancy
- Solid nodules grow faster than ground-glass

**Stability:**
- Solid nodule stable for 2 years: Likely benign
- Ground-glass nodule: May need longer follow-up (slow-growing)

## Pneumonia and Infection

### Bacterial Pneumonia

**CT Findings:**
- Consolidation (air-space opacification)
- Air bronchograms
- Lobar or segmental distribution
- Surrounding ground-glass
- Pleural effusion (parapneumonic)

**Patterns:**

| Pattern | Typical Organisms |
|---------|-------------------|
| Lobar | Streptococcus pneumoniae |
| Bronchopneumonia | Staph aureus, Gram-negatives |
| Round pneumonia | Pneumococcus (children) |

### Viral Pneumonia

**COVID-19:**
- Bilateral ground-glass opacities
- Peripheral, posterior predominance
- Crazy-paving pattern
- Consolidation in severe cases
- Subpleural sparing early

**Other Viral:**
- Bilateral ground-glass
- Interstitial pattern
- Bronchial wall thickening

### Fungal Infections

**Aspergillosis:**

| Type | Findings |
|------|----------|
| Aspergilloma | Mycetoma (fungus ball) in cavity |
| Allergic bronchopulmonary | Central bronchiectasis, mucoid impaction |
| Invasive | Nodules with halo sign, later air-crescent |
| Semi-invasive | Chronic cavitary changes |

**Pneumocystis jirovecii (PCP):**
- Bilateral ground-glass
- Perihilar distribution
- Cysts may develop
- Upper lobe predominance possible

### Tuberculosis

**Primary:**
- Lower lobe consolidation
- Hilar/mediastinal lymphadenopathy
- Pleural effusion
- Ghon complex

**Reactivation:**
- Upper lobe predominance
- Cavitation
- Tree-in-bud nodules
- Fibrosis

**Miliary:**
- Diffuse tiny nodules (1-3mm)
- Random distribution

### Lung Abscess

**CT Findings:**
- Thick-walled cavity
- Air-fluid level
- Surrounding consolidation
- May see feeding vessel

**Differentiation from Empyema:**

| Feature | Abscess | Empyema |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Shape | Round | Lentiform |
| Wall | Thick, irregular | Thin, smooth |
| Angle with chest wall | Acute | Obtuse |
| Bronchi/vessels | Terminate at wall | Displaced |

## Interstitial Lung Disease

### HRCT Patterns

#### Reticular Pattern
- Network of lines
- Septal thickening (smooth, nodular, or irregular)
- Subpleural lines
- Honeycombing (end-stage)

#### Nodular Pattern
- Centrilobular: Center of secondary lobule
- Perilymphatic: Along bronchovascular bundles, fissures
- Random: Miliary pattern

#### Ground-Glass Opacity
- Hazy increased attenuation
- Vessels still visible
- Indicates active/reversible disease

#### Consolidation
- Air-space filling
- Air bronchograms
- Obscures vessels

### Common ILD Patterns

**Usual Interstitial Pneumonia (UIP)/IPF:**
- Basal, peripheral predominance
- Honeycombing (defining feature)
- Reticulation
- Traction bronchiectasis
- Minimal ground-glass
- Craniocaudal gradient

**Nonspecific Interstitial Pneumonia (NSIP):**
- Ground-glass predominant
- Basal predominance
- Subpleural sparing
- Less honeycombing than UIP

**Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis:**
- Acute: Ground-glass, centrilobular nodules
- Chronic: Upper/mid zone predominance, air trapping, fibrosis
- Mosaic attenuation

**Sarcoidosis:**
- Perilymphatic nodules
- Upper/mid zone predominance
- Hilar/mediastinal lymphadenopathy
- Fibrosis with upper lobe retraction

**Organizing Pneumonia:**
- Consolidation
- Peripheral, subpleural
- Migratory pattern
- "Reverse halo" or atoll sign

### Secondary Lobule Anatomy

Understanding the secondary pulmonary lobule is key to HRCT interpretation:

- Lobular core: Centrilobular artery and bronchiole
- Lobular periphery: Lymphatics, pulmonary veins, interlobular septa
- Diseases affect different components

## COPD and Emphysema

### Emphysema Types

**Centrilobular (Centriacinar):**
- Most common (smoking-related)
- Upper lobe predominance
- Affects center of secondary lobule
- Round holes without walls

**Panlobular (Panacinar):**
- Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency
- Lower lobe predominance
- Uniform destruction
- Attenuated vessels

**Paraseptal:**
- Subpleural location
- Adjacent to fissures
- May cause bullae
- Associated with spontaneous pneumothorax

### Quantification

**Visual Assessment:**
- Mild: <25% of lung
- Moderate: 25-50%
- Severe: 50-75%
- Very severe: >75%

**Automated Analysis:**
- Percentage of lung below -950 HU (emphysema)
- Percentage below -856 HU on expiration (air trapping)

### Airways Disease in COPD

**Findings:**
- Bronchial wall thickening
- Mucous plugging
- Air trapping on expiration
- Mosaic attenuation

## Aortic Pathology

### Aortic Dissection

**Classification:**

| Type | Stanford | DeBakey | Involves |
|------|----------|---------|----------|
| A | A | I, II | Ascending aorta |
| B | B | III | Descending only |

**CT Findings:**
- Intimal flap
- True and false lumens
- May see entry/exit tears
- False lumen often larger
- True lumen usually has calcification on wall
- "Beak sign" in false lumen

**Complications:**
- Branch vessel involvement
- Rupture (mediastinal hematoma)
- Pericardial effusion (Type A)
- Hemothorax

### Intramural Hematoma

**CT Findings:**
- Crescent-shaped high density in aortic wall
- No intimal flap
- May progress to dissection

### Aortic Aneurysm

**Definitions:**
- Thoracic aorta >4.5cm (ascending) or >4.0cm (descending)
- True aneurysm: All layers involved
- Pseudoaneurysm: Contained rupture

**Assessment:**
- Maximum diameter (perpendicular to flow)
- Length
- Thrombus
- Branch involvement
- Signs of rupture

### Traumatic Aortic Injury

**Classic Location:**
- Aortic isthmus (distal to left subclavian)
- Ligamentum arteriosum tethers aorta

**CT Findings:**
- Intimal flap/irregularity
- Pseudoaneurysm
- Periaortic hematoma
- Active extravasation
- Pseudocoarctation

## Mediastinal Abnormalities

### Anterior Mediastinal Masses

**"4 Ts":**
- **T**hymoma/thymic tumors
- **T**eratoma/germ cell tumors
- **T**hyroid (goiter)
- Terrible **T**lymphoma

**Thymoma:**
- Smooth or lobulated
- May have calcification
- Associated with myasthenia gravis

### Middle Mediastinal Masses

- Lymphadenopathy
- Bronchogenic cyst
- Esophageal lesions
- Vascular abnormalities

### Posterior Mediastinal Masses

- Neurogenic tumors (most common)
- Meningocele
- Esophageal tumors
- Extramedullary hematopoiesis
- Descending aortic aneurysm

### Lymphadenopathy

**Normal Size:**
- <1cm short axis diameter

**Distribution Patterns:**

| Pattern | Consider |
|---------|----------|
| Symmetric bilateral hilar | Sarcoidosis |
| Asymmetric hilar | Malignancy |
| Mediastinal + hilar | Lymphoma, metastases, sarcoid |
| Calcified | Prior granulomatous disease |
| Necrotic | TB, fungal, metastases |

## Pleural Disease

### Pleural Effusion

**CT Characteristics:**
- Simple: Homogeneous fluid density
- Complex: Septations, loculations
- Empyema: Enhancing pleura, "split pleura" sign

**Differentiation:**

| Feature | Transudate | Exudate |
|---------|------------|---------|
| Density | 0-20 HU | Variable, often higher |
| Enhancement | None | May enhance |
| Loculation | Rare | Common |
| Associated findings | Cardiac disease | Infection, malignancy |

### Pleural Thickening

**Benign:**
- Smooth
- Uniform
- May be calcified (asbestos, prior infection)

**Malignant (Mesothelioma):**
- Nodular
- >1cm thickness
- Circumferential
- Mediastinal pleural involvement
- Volume loss

### Pneumothorax

**CT Findings:**
- Air in pleural space
- Collapsed lung
- May see point of visceral pleural detachment

**Tension Pneumothorax:**
- Mediastinal shift
- Flattened hemidiaphragm
- Compressed contralateral lung

## Trauma

### Pulmonary Contusion

**CT Findings:**
- Ground-glass or consolidation
- Non-segmental distribution
- Adjacent to injury site
- May take hours to develop
- Clears within 72 hours-1 week

### Pulmonary Laceration

**CT Findings:**
- Lung tear (cavity)
- May contain air, blood, or both
- Pneumatocele (air-filled)
- Hematocele (blood-filled)

### Tracheobronchial Injury

**CT Findings:**
- Pneumomediastinum
- Persistent pneumothorax despite chest tube
- "Fallen lung" sign
- Bronchial discontinuity

### Diaphragm Rupture

**CT Findings:**
- Discontinuity of diaphragm
- Herniated abdominal contents
- "Dependent viscera" sign
- "Collar" sign (waist of herniated organ)
- Left side more common (right protected by liver)

## Summary Checklist

### Systematic Review

- [ ] Lung parenchyma (both windows)
- [ ] Airways (trachea to segmental)
- [ ] Pleural spaces
- [ ] Mediastinum and lymph nodes
- [ ] Heart and pericardium
- [ ] Vessels (aorta, PA, veins)
- [ ] Chest wall and bones
- [ ] Upper abdomen
- [ ] Compared to priors

### Critical Findings

Report immediately:
- Pulmonary embolism (especially central or with RV strain)
- Aortic dissection
- Tension pneumothorax
- Active hemorrhage
- New malignancy with significant findings
