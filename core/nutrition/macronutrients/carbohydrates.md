# Carbohydrates

## Overview
Carbohydrates are organic compounds consisting of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms, serving as the primary energy source for cellular metabolism. They provide 4 kilocalories per gram and are essential for brain function, physical activity, and proper metabolic regulation.

---

## Level 1: Basic Understanding

### What Are Carbohydrates?
Carbohydrates are sugars, starches, and fibers found in foods. They are your body's main source of quick energy.

**Simple Carbohydrates (Sugars)**
- Found in fruits, candy, sodas, and table sugar
- Give you quick energy but don't last long
- Examples: glucose, fructose, sucrose

**Complex Carbohydrates (Starches)**
- Found in bread, pasta, rice, and potatoes
- Give you longer-lasting energy
- Take more time to digest

**Fiber**
- Found in vegetables, fruits, and whole grains
- Helps your digestive system work properly
- Cannot be fully digested

### Why You Need Carbohydrates
- Provide energy for your brain
- Fuel your muscles during exercise
- Help your body work properly
- Keep you feeling full

### Good Sources
- Whole grains (brown rice, oatmeal, whole wheat bread)
- Fruits and vegetables
- Beans and lentils
- Dairy products

## Level 2: Intermediate Understanding

### Classification of Carbohydrates

**Monosaccharides (Single Sugars)**
| Sugar | Source | Sweetness |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| Glucose | Blood sugar, fruits | Reference (100) |
| Fructose | Fruits, honey | Very sweet (173) |
| Galactose | Dairy products | Less sweet (32) |

**Disaccharides (Double Sugars)**
| Sugar | Composition | Source |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Sucrose | Glucose + Fructose | Table sugar, sugarcane |
| Lactose | Glucose + Galactose | Milk and dairy |
| Maltose | Glucose + Glucose | Malted grains, beer |

**Polysaccharides (Complex Carbohydrates)**
- **Starch**: Plant storage form (potatoes, grains)
- **Glycogen**: Animal storage form (liver, muscle)
- **Fiber**: Indigestible plant material (cellulose, pectin)

### Carbohydrate Digestion
1. **Mouth**: Salivary amylase begins breaking down starch
2. **Stomach**: Acid stops amylase activity
3. **Small Intestine**: Pancreatic amylase completes starch digestion
4. **Brush Border**: Final enzymes (maltase, sucrase, lactase) break down disaccharides
5. **Absorption**: Monosaccharides absorbed into bloodstream

### Glycemic Index (GI)
Measures how quickly foods raise blood sugar:
- **Low GI (0-55)**: Slow digestion, steady energy
- **Medium GI (56-69)**: Moderate blood sugar rise
- **High GI (70+)**: Rapid blood sugar spike

| Food | GI Value |
|------|----------|
| White bread | 75 |
| Brown rice | 50 |
| Apple | 36 |
| Glucose | 100 |

### Daily Recommendations
- 45-65% of total daily calories
- About 130 grams minimum for brain function
- 25-30 grams of fiber daily

## Level 3: Advanced Understanding

### Molecular Structure and Chemistry

**Monosaccharide Structure**
- General formula: (CH2O)n where n = 3-7
- Classified by carbon number: trioses (3C), tetroses (4C), pentoses (5C), hexoses (6C)
- Aldose vs. ketose: aldehyde group vs. ketone group
- D and L isomers: stereochemistry at the chiral center farthest from the carbonyl group

**Ring Formation**
- Hemiacetal/hemiketal formation in aqueous solution
- Pyranose (6-membered) vs. furanose (5-membered) rings
- Alpha and beta anomers: configuration at the anomeric carbon
- Mutarotation: interconversion between alpha and beta forms

**Glycosidic Bonds**
- Alpha-1,4 linkages: digestible (starch)
- Beta-1,4 linkages: indigestible (cellulose)
- Alpha-1,6 linkages: branching points in glycogen and amylopectin
- Configuration determines digestibility and function

### Starch Structure
**Amylose (20-30% of starch)**
- Linear chain of alpha-1,4 linked glucose
- Forms helical structure
- More resistant to digestion
- Creates gel texture when cooled

**Amylopectin (70-80% of starch)**
- Branched structure with alpha-1,4 backbone
- Alpha-1,6 branches every 24-30 glucose units
- More rapidly digested
- Provides thickening properties

### Glycemic Load
GL = (GI x carbohydrate content per serving) / 100

More accurate than GI alone:
- **Low GL**: <10
- **Medium GL**: 11-19
- **High GL**: >20

### Factors Affecting Glycemic Response
1. **Food processing**: More processing = higher GI
2. **Fiber content**: More fiber = lower GI
3. **Fat and protein**: Slow gastric emptying
4. **Cooking method**: Al dente pasta < overcooked
5. **Ripeness**: Riper fruit = higher GI
6. **Food combinations**: Mixed meals lower GI

### Resistant Starch
Starch that escapes digestion in the small intestine:
- **Type 1**: Physically inaccessible (whole grains)
- **Type 2**: Native granule structure (raw potato, green banana)
- **Type 3**: Retrograded starch (cooled cooked starch)
- **Type 4**: Chemically modified starch
- **Type 5**: Amylose-lipid complexes

Benefits: Acts like fiber, feeds beneficial gut bacteria

## Level 4: Expert Understanding

### Carbohydrate Digestion Mechanisms

**Salivary Alpha-Amylase (EC 3.2.1.1)**
- Optimal pH: 6.7-7.0
- Cleaves alpha-1,4 glycosidic bonds
- Cannot hydrolyze alpha-1,6 branch points
- Products: maltose, maltotriose, alpha-limit dextrins
- Activity time: Limited due to gastric acid inactivation

**Pancreatic Alpha-Amylase**
- Secreted as active enzyme (not zymogen)
- Optimal pH: 7.0-7.4
- Same specificity as salivary amylase
- Much higher concentration and activity time

**Brush Border Enzymes**
| Enzyme | Substrate | Products | Location |
|--------|-----------|----------|----------|
| Maltase | Maltose | 2 Glucose | Duodenum, Jejunum |
| Isomaltase | Alpha-1,6 bonds | Glucose | Jejunum |
| Sucrase | Sucrose | Glucose + Fructose | Jejunum |
| Lactase | Lactose | Glucose + Galactose | Jejunum |
| Trehalase | Trehalose | 2 Glucose | Jejunum |

**Sucrase-Isomaltase Complex**
- Single gene product, cleaved into two subunits
- Anchored to brush border membrane
- Sucrase subunit: hydrolyzes sucrose and maltose
- Isomaltase subunit: hydrolyzes alpha-1,6 bonds and maltose

### Monosaccharide Absorption

**SGLT1 (Sodium-Glucose Cotransporter 1)**
- Active transport for glucose and galactose
- Couples sodium gradient to sugar uptake
- Stoichiometry: 2 Na+ : 1 glucose
- Located on apical membrane of enterocytes
- Electrogenic: creates positive current

**GLUT5**
- Facilitated diffusion for fructose
- Fructose-specific transporter
- Located on apical membrane
- Capacity-limited: excess fructose causes malabsorption

**GLUT2**
- Basolateral membrane transporter
- Transports glucose, galactose, and fructose
- Low affinity, high capacity
- Also recruits to apical membrane at high glucose concentrations

### Insulin-Stimulated Glucose Uptake

**GLUT4 Translocation**
1. Insulin binds receptor (tyrosine kinase)
2. Receptor autophosphorylation
3. IRS-1/2 phosphorylation
4. PI3-kinase activation
5. Akt (PKB) phosphorylation
6. AS160 phosphorylation
7. GLUT4 vesicle translocation to membrane
8. Glucose uptake increases 10-20 fold

### Glycemic Variability
**Continuous Glucose Monitoring Parameters**
- Time in Range (TIR): % time 70-180 mg/dL
- Time Above Range (TAR): % time >180 mg/dL
- Time Below Range (TBR): % time <70 mg/dL
- Glucose Coefficient of Variation (CV): Standard deviation/mean
- Mean Amplitude of Glycemic Excursions (MAGE)

## Level 5: Research/Professional Level

### Carbohydrate Sensing and Signaling

**Cellular Glucose Sensing Mechanisms**
1. **Glucokinase (Hexokinase IV)**
   - Low affinity for glucose (Km ~8 mM)
   - Not inhibited by glucose-6-phosphate
   - Acts as pancreatic beta-cell glucose sensor
   - Regulatory protein GKRP sequesters GK in nucleus
   - High glucose releases GK to cytoplasm

2. **AMPK (AMP-activated Protein Kinase)**
   - Cellular energy sensor
   - Activated by increased AMP:ATP ratio
   - Stimulates glucose uptake via GLUT4
   - Enhances fatty acid oxidation
   - Inhibits anabolic pathways

3. **mTORC1 Signaling**
   - Nutrient-sensitive protein complex
   - Activated by glucose availability
   - Promotes protein synthesis and growth
   - Inhibits autophagy
   - Dysregulation linked to metabolic disease

**Incretin Hormones and Glucose Homeostasis**

*GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1)*
- Secreted by L-cells in ileum and colon
- Stimulates insulin secretion (glucose-dependent)
- Inhibits glucagon secretion
- Slows gastric emptying
- Promotes satiety
- Half-life: 2 minutes (DPP-4 degradation)

*GIP (Glucose-Dependent Insulinotropic Polypeptide)*
- Secreted by K-cells in duodenum
- Potentiates glucose-stimulated insulin secretion
- Promotes lipogenesis in adipose tissue
- Does not inhibit glucagon secretion
- Half-life: 5-7 minutes

### Advanced Glycemic Assessment

**Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT) Interpretation**
| Time | Normal | Impaired Glucose Tolerance | Diabetes |
|------|--------|---------------------------|----------|
| Fasting | <100 mg/dL | 100-125 mg/dL | ≥126 mg/dL |
| 2-hour | <140 mg/dL | 140-199 mg/dL | ≥200 mg/dL |

**Insulinogenic Index**
(Insulin 30 min - Insulin 0 min) / (Glucose 30 min - Glucose 0 min)
- Measures early-phase insulin secretion
- Reduced in prediabetes and T2DM

**Matsuda Index (Whole-Body Insulin Sensitivity)**
10,000 / √(Fasting glucose × Fasting insulin × Mean glucose × Mean insulin)
- Correlates with hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp
- Values <2.5 indicate insulin resistance

**HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment)**
(Fasting insulin × Fasting glucose) / 405
- Simple insulin resistance calculation
- Values >2.5-3.0 indicate insulin resistance

### Carbohydrate Quality and Disease Risk

**Fiber and Microbiome Interactions**
- Fermentation produces short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs)
- Acetate: Peripheral tissue metabolism
- Propionate: Gluconeogenesis inhibition
- Butyrate: Colonocyte energy, anti-inflammatory
- Microbiome composition affects SCFA production
- Individual variation in glycemic response

**Advanced Glycation End Products (AGEs)**
Formation: Maillard reaction between reducing sugars and amino groups
- Intracellular AGE formation: methylglyoxal from glycolysis
- Receptor for AGEs (RAGE): pro-inflammatory signaling
- AGE crosslinks: tissue stiffness, vascular dysfunction
- Dietary AGEs: high-heat processed foods

**Carbohydrate-Insulin Model of Obesity**
Hypothesis: High-GI diets promote hyperinsulinemia → fat storage → energy deficit → increased appetite
Critiques: Metabolic ward studies show similar weight loss regardless of macronutrient composition when calories matched

### Precision Nutrition Approaches

**Predictive Glycemic Response Models**
Machine learning incorporating:
- Meal composition (carbohydrates, fats, proteins, fiber)
- Microbiome profile (Prevotella:Bacteroides ratio)
- Anthropometrics (BMI, waist circumference)
- Activity level and timing
- Sleep quality and duration
- Previous meal effects

**Chronocarbohydrate Considerations**
- Morning glucose tolerance typically better
- Evening carbohydrates may promote glycogen synthesis
- Meal timing affects circadian metabolic rhythms
- Time-restricted eating modifies carbohydrate handling

### Clinical Applications

**Carbohydrate Counting for Diabetes**
- 1 carb choice = 15 g carbohydrate
- Insulin-to-carb ratio: units insulin per carb choice
- Correction factor: mg/dL drop per unit insulin
- Advanced ratio adjustment: fat and protein counting

**Low-Carbohydrate Diet Protocols**
| Diet Type | Carbs (g/day) | % Calories |
|-----------|---------------|------------|
| Moderate LC | 100-150 | 26-44% |
| Low-carb | 50-100 | 10-26% |
| Very low-carb | 20-50 | <10% |
| Ketogenic | <20-50 | <10% |

**Carbohydrate Loading for Athletes**
- Classic protocol: Depletion + 3 days 10-12 g/kg/day
- Modified protocol: Taper + 3 days 8-10 g/kg/day
- Increases muscle glycogen 100-200%
- Most beneficial for endurance events >90 minutes

## Clinical Considerations

### Carbohydrate-Related Disorders

**Lactose Intolerance**
- Primary: Lactase non-persistence (70% of world population)
- Secondary: Intestinal disease damage
- Congenital: Rare autosomal recessive
- Treatment: Lactase supplements, lactose-free products

**Sucrase-Isomaltase Deficiency**
- Congenital or acquired
- Sucrose and starch malabsorption
- Treatment: Enzyme replacement (sacrosidase)

**Glucose-Galactose Malabsorption**
- SGLT1 mutation
- Neonatal diarrhea
- Treatment: Fructose-based formula

**Glycogen Storage Diseases**
| Type | Enzyme Deficiency | Features |
|------|-------------------|----------|
| 0 | Glycogen synthase | Hypoglycemia, ketosis |
| I (von Gierke) | Glucose-6-phosphatase | Hepatomegaly, hypoglycemia |
| II (Pompe) | Acid maltase | Cardiomyopathy, myopathy |
| III (Cori) | Debranching enzyme | Hepatomegaly, hypoglycemia |
| IV (Andersen) | Branching enzyme | Cirrhosis |
| V (McArdle) | Muscle phosphorylase | Exercise intolerance |
| VI (Hers) | Liver phosphorylase | Hepatomegaly, mild hypoglycemia |

## Key Takeaways

1. Carbohydrates are the body's preferred and most efficient energy source
2. Quality matters: whole grains, fruits, vegetables > refined sugars
3. Glycemic index and load help predict blood sugar response
4. Individual glycemic response varies significantly
5. Fiber provides metabolic and microbiome benefits
6. Carbohydrate needs vary by activity level, health status, and goals
7. Both extremes (very high and very low carbohydrate) have tradeoffs
